## Blessing

A blessing is a short spoken or written invocation of favor, protection, or grace over a person, gathering, meal, or undertaking. Its defining move is directional: the blessing faces the recipient and calls good upon them. Words flow toward a person, a table, or a new venture from one who holds recognized authority, care, or community standing. The officiant, elder, parent, or pastor speaks; the recipient receives. That directionality is what separates the blessing from almost every other devotional format.

The blessing typically invokes a source - God, grace, love - as the power behind the words, but it does not address that source directly. The invoking phrase is folded into the pronouncement: "May God hold you in the palm of his hand" calls upon God while speaking to you. This keeps the recipient at the center. The blessing is not a report about God's character; it is an act of conferring. Even in secular forms - a graduation send-off, a retirement farewell - the same structure holds: favor is pronounced over the person, not discussed in the abstract.

### Canonical template

```
[Recipient Address - optional]
  Name or identify who or what is being blessed.
  ("To you, [name]," "Over this meal," "For this house,")

[Invocation of source - optional]
  Name the ground from which the blessing flows, without addressing it directly.
  ("By God's grace," "In the presence of all gathered here,")

[Pronouncement - required]
  Second-person declaration of favor, protection, or grace over the recipient:
  "May you..."
  "Be blessed in..."
  "You are held by..."

[Specific naming - required]
  Name the person, season, undertaking, or quality being blessed.
  The more specific, the more the blessing lands.

[Sealing close - optional]
  "Amen," "Go in peace," "So be it," or a final line of grace.
```

### When to use

Opening or closing a worship service, retreat, or community gathering with a spoken or written blessing, sending someone into a new season (graduation, marriage, new role, deployment, or retirement), pronouncing favor over a meal, a home, or a new undertaking before it begins, writing a blessing for a child, congregant, or friend at a significant life threshold, marking a liturgical occasion with words of grace particular to that season or community.

### When not to use

When the address is directed toward the divine rather than toward a human recipient (use prayer instead), professional or secular contexts where pronouncing favor over someone would feel presumptuous or mismatched to the relationship, when the goal is to explain, teach, or reflect on faith concepts rather than to confer grace on a recipient.

### Pairs well with

`pastoral`, `caregiver`, `reverent`, `warm`, `encouraging`

### Often confused with

**prayer**: A prayer is a written address to the divine - petition, thanksgiving, confession, lament, or praise - directed at God in second person, with the human reader as witness rather than recipient. A blessing, by contrast, directs its words at the human recipient, pronouncing favor over them; the divine (when invoked) appears as the source of that favor, not as the audience. The difference is direction of speech: prayer speaks to God, while a blessing speaks to or over the person being blessed.
