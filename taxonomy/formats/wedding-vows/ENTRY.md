## Wedding Vows

Wedding vows are the personal promises spoken by one partner to the other during a marriage ceremony. They are first-person commitments, specific to the relationship, addressed directly to the beloved. The format is intimate even when the room is full: every word travels to one person, not to the crowd. The gathered guests are not the audience - they are the witnesses.

The structure of wedding vows follows a recognizable arc. An opening grounds the speaker in the specific relationship being formalized: who this person is, what this moment means. The body carries the commitments themselves - what the speaker promises to do, to be, and to remain through the life ahead. The close lands on the enduring scope of the promise, invoking permanence and the weight of a lifelong commitment.

What distinguishes vows from other ceremonial writing is their direct address and their stakes. They are not a tribute to the partner delivered to the room; they are a promise delivered to the partner, witnessed by the room. Every language choice should feel like something the speaker would say to the beloved in private, even knowing others will hear it.

Typical length is 100-300 words per partner. Shorter risks feeling thin relative to the gravity of the moment; longer risks burying the promises inside narrative.

### Canonical template

```
# Wedding Vows: [Your Name] to [Partner's Name]

## Opening
[Name your partner and anchor the moment in the specific relationship. "Today, in front of
everyone we love..." or "Standing here with you, I want to say..." - orient yourself to the
person, not to the room.]

## The Promises
[The commitments themselves - first-person, direct, specific to this relationship. "I promise
to..." structured in 3-5 distinct vows. Name particular qualities of the relationship or the
person, not only generic virtues.]

## The Close
[Land on the enduring nature of the promise. Invoke permanence and the scope of the commitment
- what you are choosing through all that comes. Ends with the speaker's own closing words to
the partner, not an invitation to the room.]
```

### When to use

Writing personal vows to speak during a wedding ceremony; renewing vows at a significant anniversary with promises that reflect what the relationship has become; crafting vows for a ceremony where the officiant invites personalized rather than scripted language; when the couple wants their commitment to feel particular to them, not borrowed from a generic template; any ceremonial context where first-person direct address to the partner is the expected form.

### When not to use

Toasts, speeches, or tributes addressed to the room about the couple rather than to the partner directly; formal legal documents capturing the terms of a partnership or domestic union; settings where the speaker is addressing the gathered crowd rather than making a direct promise to a specific person.

### Pairs well with

`storyteller`, `pastoral`, `warm`, `reverent`, `celebratory`

### Often confused with

**toast**: A toast is a short, warm tribute built for the room - it speaks to the assembled guests to celebrate a person or occasion, and closes with a spoken invitation to raise a glass, which is the structural signal that transforms the monologue into a shared ritual. Wedding vows are addressed to the partner alone. In vows, the gathered guests are witnesses to a promise, not the intended audience of a tribute; the close belongs to the speaker and the partner, not to the room.
