## Manifesto

A manifesto is a public declaration of beliefs, principles, and intentions - a statement of what a person, group, or movement stands for and aims to do. It is aspirational and identity-defining, written to rally the like-minded and announce a stance to the world. The manifesto does not wait to be invited into a debate; it opens one. Its unit is the principle, not the paragraph.

The canonical structure of a manifesto moves from premise to principles to call. The writer opens by naming the condition or worldview that makes this document necessary - the thing being declared against or found insufficient. The body states the core beliefs: what the movement holds to be true, what it rejects, and what it commits to. The close is an exhortation - what the writer or movement intends to do, and what is asked of those who stand with them. This arc from premise to commitment to call distinguishes the manifesto from formats that argue, survey, or instruct.

Unlike an op-ed, which argues one clear position on a specific timely issue for a publication's readership, a manifesto declares a whole worldview or program. Where the op-ed advances a single claim and then exits, the manifesto lays down a system of beliefs and says: this is who we are. It is broader, more foundational, and concerned with values and identity rather than winning a single argument on a single question.

A manifesto's power comes from its willingness to commit fully. It names what it stands against as well as what it stands for, makes claims without qualifying hedges, and invites readers to choose a side. That commitment is also its risk: a manifesto that retreats into concessions or softens its principles to avoid alienating anyone has abandoned the form.

### Canonical template

```
[Opening: the premise or critique - the condition or worldview that makes this manifesto
necessary. 1-2 sentences that name what is being declared against or what has been found
insufficient.]

[Worldview: the core beliefs that follow from the premise. Stated as principles, not as
evidence-based arguments. 3-7 items or paragraphs.]

[What we stand against: the alternative, the status quo, or the opposing force - named
plainly, without equivocation.]

[What we stand for: the positive commitments, stated without hedge or qualification.]

[Call: what the movement or writer intends to do, and what is asked of those who are with us.]
```

### When to use

- Declaring a movement's, organization's, or person's founding beliefs and values
- Announcing a new stance, mission, or philosophy to a public audience
- Rallying aligned readers around a shared identity or cause
- Establishing what a project or initiative stands for at its founding or relaunch
- Responding to a status quo the writer rejects by naming the alternative publicly

### When not to use

- When the goal is to argue a specific timely claim for a publication's readership - use an op-ed instead
- When readers need practical instruction, steps, or actionable guidance rather than a declaration of belief
- When the context calls for collaborative deliberation rather than a public announcement of position

### Pairs well with

`columnist`, `executive`, `resolute`, `confident`

### Often confused with

**op-ed**: An op-ed is a short argued opinion piece - typically 600 to 800 words - written for a publication's readership to advance one clear position on a timely issue. Where an op-ed argues one specific claim and then exits, a manifesto declares a whole worldview or program: it is broader, more foundational, and concerned with values and identity rather than winning a single argument on a single question. The op-ed's unit is the claim and its supporting evidence; the manifesto's unit is the belief and its implications.
