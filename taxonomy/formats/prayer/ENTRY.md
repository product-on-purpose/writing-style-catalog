## Prayer

A prayer is written speech directed to the divine rather than to a human reader. The writer turns toward God and speaks - asking, thanking, confessing, lamenting, or praising. The human who later reads or voices the words is a witness, not the intended recipient. That inversion is the format's defining move: the audience is not in the room.

Prayer takes one of five classical postures. Petition asks for something. Thanksgiving names what has been given. Confession brings failure before grace. Lament speaks grief or protest honestly to God, refusing to tidy it up. Praise ascribes worth to the divine, with nothing asked in return. The posture determines the emotional register, but all five share one formal commitment: the writer addresses God in the second person and does not slip into speaking about God as though God were absent.

Because the audience is divine rather than human, prayer permits a directness and vulnerability that other devotional writing withholds. A devotional reflection tells the human reader what the author has learned about faith; a prayer lets the reader overhear a soul speaking to God. That distinction governs everything: vocabulary softens into intimacy, argumentation gives way to surrender or longing, and the rhetorical goal shifts from persuasion to presence.

### Canonical template

```
[Opening Address]
  Name or title for the divine (e.g., "Lord," "Gracious God," "Father")

[Posture - choose one and sustain it]
  Petition:     "I ask... grant... help... lead..."
  Thanksgiving: "Thank you for... I am grateful for..."
  Confession:   "I have... forgive... restore..."
  Lament:       "How long... why... see my grief..."
  Praise:       "You are... worthy... holy..."

[Body]
  The honest substance of the posture, addressed to God in second person.
  Be specific: the actual need, the actual gratitude, the actual grief.

[Close]
  A brief phrase that ends the address: "Amen," "In your name," or a final
  line of praise that returns focus to the divine.
```

### When to use

Writing a prayer for a worship service, liturgy, or gathering, crafting a prayer for a specific occasion such as healing, grief, celebration, or a new year, opening or closing a devotional resource with a written prayer, giving a congregation or community words they can speak or pray together, writing a prayer that stands alone as a literary or liturgical artifact.

### When not to use

Content intended to teach, reflect, or explain faith concepts to a reader (use devotional-reflection instead), secular contexts where addressing the divine would be alienating or inappropriate, writing where the author's own commentary or insight is the primary value delivered.

### Pairs well with

`pastoral`, `caregiver`, `reverent`, `warm`

### Often confused with

**devotional-entry**: A devotional entry reflects about faith for a human reader, teaching or encouraging through scripture, personal reflection, and application takeaways. It speaks about God and about spiritual life toward the reader. A prayer, by contrast, addresses God directly from start to finish; the human reader is an observer, not the audience. The difference is not subject matter but direction of address.
