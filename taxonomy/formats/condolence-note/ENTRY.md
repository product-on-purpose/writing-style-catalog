## Condolence Note

A condolence note is a brief, private written message of sympathy sent to someone who has lost a loved one. It names the loss, offers genuine presence rather than solutions, often shares one specific memory of the person who died, and asks nothing in return. Unlike a eulogy, which is delivered aloud to a gathered room and makes the deceased present through public tribute, a condolence note is written to the bereaved themselves - its audience is the grieving person, and its work is to let them feel accompanied.

The format has almost no formal structure, which makes it harder than it appears. A condolence note is not a list of comforting ideas or a spiritual message about what happens after death. It is a human reaching across to another human in the specific fact of their loss. The most powerful element is usually one concrete detail: a memory of the person who died, a quality only someone who knew them would name, or a recognition of exactly what this particular loss means to this particular person.

Brevity is a feature, not a limitation. A condolence note that runs to several paragraphs often reveals that the writer is processing their own feelings rather than showing up for the reader. The note asks nothing of the bereaved - no response required, no gratitude expected. It lands, it witnesses, and it stops.

### Canonical template

```
[Brief acknowledgment of the loss - name the person who died]

[One specific memory, quality, or detail about the deceased or the relationship]

[A simple statement of presence or availability - nothing required of the reader]

[Sign-off without demand or expectation]
```

### When to use

When someone you know has lost a loved one and you want to acknowledge the loss without requiring anything of them; when the relationship is close enough that silence would feel like absence but a phone call might feel like a burden; when you have a specific memory or observation about the deceased that the bereaved might not have heard; when you want to reach someone in the first days or weeks of grief after formal gatherings have ended; when a written record of acknowledgment matters and physical presence is not possible.

### When not to use

When you are delivering a tribute to a gathered room at a funeral or memorial service - use a eulogy instead; when the relationship is close enough that a direct phone call or physical presence is more appropriate; when you have no genuine personal connection to the bereaved or the deceased.

### Pairs well with

`caregiver`, `pastoral`, `empathetic`, `warm`, `reverent`

### Often confused with

**eulogy**: A eulogy is a tribute delivered aloud to a gathered room at a funeral or memorial service; it honors the deceased by making them present one more time through specific, concrete memory, and it addresses the audience as a shared community of grief. A condolence note is written privately to the bereaved person alone - it does not address a room, it does not serve as public tribute, and it does not perform memory for an audience. The distinction is not just medium (spoken vs. written) but audience and purpose: a eulogy belongs to the gathered room, a condolence note belongs to the person reading it alone.
