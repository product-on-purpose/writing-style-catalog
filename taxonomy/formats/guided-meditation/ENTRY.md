## Guided Meditation

A guided meditation is a script written to be read aloud or recorded that leads a listener
through a sequence of attention - breath, body, image, or awareness - in real time, with
pacing and pauses built in. It speaks to the practitioner directly, directing their inner
experience moment by moment. The writer is a guide whose sole job is to carry the listener
into an interior space, not to demonstrate insight or explain the practice.

The structure is functional: an opening that invites the listener to arrive (settle, close
eyes, breathe); a middle that moves attention deliberately through breath, body sensation,
image, or awareness; and a close that gently returns the listener to the room. Each
instruction is a soft imperative - "notice," "allow," "imagine," "bring your attention to" -
rather than a request or an explanation. Transition sentences serve as pacing cues, marking
the shift from one focus to the next, and white space or notated pauses give the reader room
to actually do what is asked.

Unlike a prayer, which is addressed to the divine and voiced by the speaker upward toward God,
a guided meditation is addressed directly to the listener and directs their attention inward.
Its audience is the person being guided, not a divine recipient. The aim is a state of
awareness - calm, presence, body attunement - not an exchange with God or a petition sent
heavenward.

### Canonical template

```
[Arrival / Opening Invitation]
  "Find a comfortable position... close your eyes... take a breath..."
  Settle the listener. Name the intention without explaining it.

[Attention Sequence]
  [Breath anchor]
    "Notice the breath... inhale... exhale... let go..."
  [Body scan or image]
    "Bring attention to [area / scene / sensation]... notice what is here..."
  [Deepening]
    "Allow yourself to rest in... imagine... stay with..."

[Transition cues]
  Brief pacing sentences: "When you are ready... gently shift your attention to..."
  [Optional pause notation: (pause 5 to 10 seconds)]

[Return / Close]
  "Slowly begin to notice the room around you... take a full breath...
  when you are ready, open your eyes."
```

### When to use

Writing a script to lead a group through a centering exercise before a meeting, workshop, or retreat, creating a recorded audio meditation for a wellness, spiritual formation, or mindfulness resource, opening a contemplative worship service or spiritual direction session with a guided breath or body awareness exercise, accompanying a visualization exercise in a coaching or therapeutic context, leading a group through an imaginative Scripture reading or lectio divina style practice.

### When not to use

Content that explains what meditation is or teaches concepts about mindfulness to a reader (use an essay or devotional entry instead), writing addressed to God rather than to the listener (use prayer instead), high-urgency or high-information contexts where the listener needs to act quickly, not settle inward.

### Pairs well with

`pastoral`, `caregiver`, `warm`, `reverent`, `procedural`

### Often confused with

**prayer**: A prayer is a written address to the divine - petition, thanksgiving, confession, or praise. The writer turns toward God and speaks in second person throughout, with the human who reads or voices the words serving as witness, not as the intended audience. A guided meditation, by contrast, addresses the listener directly and uses soft-imperative second-person language to move the practitioner's attention inward. The audience is the person being guided; the aim is a state of awareness, not an exchange with God. Where prayer sends speech upward to the divine, a guided meditation carries the listener inward to their own experience.
