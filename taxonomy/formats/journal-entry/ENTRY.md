## Journal Entry

A journal entry is written for an audience of one: the writer. It records what happened and, more importantly, what the writer made of it - thinking on the page, unguarded, without the performance that a reader would invite. Its value is the honesty that privacy permits. The format presupposes no approval, no explanation, no persuasion. It simply asks: what is actually true for me right now?

The dated entry is the format's structural spine. The date anchors the reflection to a specific moment and turns the journal into a record across time - a place to return to and see how thinking has moved. But the date is not a log entry; it is a frame. The work of a journal entry is the inner layer: what the event or encounter or feeling surfaces, not merely what occurred.

Unlike a devotional entry, a journal entry carries no spiritual frame and addresses no third party. It is the self working something out on the page. The reflection may be tentative, circling, or unresolved - that quality is not a defect. A journal entry earns its value by being honest rather than polished.

### Canonical template

```
[Date]

[What happened - the event, encounter, or thought that opened this entry]

[What I made of it - what it surfaced, what I noticed, what question it raised]

[Where I land - what I want to carry forward, or what remains unresolved and why]
```

### When to use

Processing a significant experience while it is still close, working through a decision or conflict by writing without a reader in mind, building a sustained record of your thinking across time, practicing honesty on the page without the pressure of an audience, capturing the felt texture of a day or encounter, not just its events.

### When not to use

When the writing has an intended reader, even a private or sympathetic one; when the goal is to publish or share the reflection at any point; when a structured output is required - report, memo, recap, or similar.

### Pairs well with

`columnist`, `storyteller`, `confessional`, `candid`, `chronological-narrative`

### Often confused with

**devotional-entry**: A devotional entry is written for a human reader, usually anchored to scripture or a spiritual observation, and moves toward personal application. It may include a prayer, but it is not itself direct address to God from start to finish. A journal entry carries none of that frame. It is written for no reader at all, assumes no witness, and has no spiritual anchor. Where a devotional entry organizes reflection around a received text and moves toward shared application, a journal entry simply follows wherever honest self-inquiry leads.
