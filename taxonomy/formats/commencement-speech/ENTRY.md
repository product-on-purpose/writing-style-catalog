## Commencement Speech

A commencement speech is delivered by a guest speaker at a graduation ceremony, addressing the graduates at the threshold between one chapter and what comes next. The speaker has been invited to mark the occasion with reflection, hard-won wisdom, and a send-off - not to receive recognition, but to confer it. The ceremony belongs to the graduates; the speaker's role is to honor that moment and equip those departing with something worth carrying forward.

The format moves in a recognizable arc. The opening locates the moment - naming what this day means, acknowledging the graduates, and establishing the speaker's connection to the occasion. The middle carries the substantive gift: a story, a lesson, or a truth the speaker learned the harder way than the graduates need to. The close delivers the charge and the send-off - what the speaker hopes the graduates will do with what they have just earned. A commencement speech earns its ending by building to it; the charge lands only when the speech has done the work to make it matter.

The speaker is a guest and a giver, not a recipient. That orientation shapes every structural choice: the wisdom points toward the graduates, the stories illuminate their journey as much as the speaker's own, and the close is about what they will become rather than what the speaker has been. Unlike an acceptance speech, where the speaker is the named recipient of an award and directs gratitude outward to those who helped them, a commencement speaker holds no recognition to receive - they are there to give it.

### When to use

Speaking as the invited address at a graduation, commencement, or completion ceremony; honoring a class of graduates with counsel and reflection on behalf of those who came before; welcoming a cohort of new professionals or alumni into a field or community at the moment of their entry; writing a ceremonial speech in which the speaker's role is to give wisdom to the honorees, not to receive it; closing a long program of student accomplishment with a capstone address from an outside voice.

### When not to use

When you are the person receiving the honor - use acceptance-speech instead; when the audience is not a graduating or transitioning group with a shared threshold to cross; when the occasion calls for a short tribute to one named individual rather than counsel to a group.

### Pairs well with

`storyteller`, `coach`, `warm`, `encouraging`, `narrative-case-study`

### Often confused with

**acceptance-speech**: An acceptance speech is delivered by the person receiving an award or honor - the speaker is the named recipient who takes the podium to thank those who made the moment possible and reflect on what the recognition means. Gratitude flows outward from the honoree toward collaborators, mentors, and institutions. A commencement speaker holds the opposite position: they are a guest, not a recipient, and the recognition belongs entirely to the graduates. Where an acceptance speech distributes credit outward from the person being celebrated, a commencement address confers wisdom and a charge on people who are about to begin - the speaker gives rather than receives, and the close is a send-off rather than a reflection on an award.
