## Thank-You Note

A thank-you note does one thing: it thanks a specific person for a specific thing, in a way that makes them feel the gratitude rather than just receive it. Its power is in naming the particular - what they did, what it cost them, what it made possible - not in length or eloquence. Unlike a general email or a form acknowledgment, the thank-you note has a single purpose and asks for nothing in return.

What separates a thank-you note from a polite acknowledgment is specificity. Saying "thank you for your support" tells someone you noticed they existed. Saying "I would not have made it to that interview without the practice session you ran with me at 10pm on a Tuesday" tells them you are still thinking about what they did. The particular is what moves people. Vague gratitude is social hygiene; named gratitude is a gift.

The note earns its form by staying short. A thank-you note that runs to four paragraphs has usually drifted into recounting the full story or attempting to match the gesture with equal words. The gift of the form is attention paid to the recipient: their action, their character, their impact. When it turns inward to the writer's feelings at length, it loses that.

### Canonical template

```
[Salutation - first name or name with greeting]

[Name the specific thing they did - one sentence, precise.]

[Name what it made possible or what it meant - one honest sentence.]

[Optional: one thing you noticed about them in doing it.]

[Closing - warm, no call to action]
```

### When to use

Thanking someone who did something specific and meaningful for you, acknowledging a person who went out of their way or spent time they did not have to spend, following a gift or introduction or act of mentorship, after an event where someone hosted or organized in a way that made a real difference, when a specific moment in a professional or personal relationship deserves to be named.

### When not to use

When you cannot name anything specific, when the context calls for a formal acknowledgment where warmth would seem out of register, when you are also making a request.

### Pairs well with

`caregiver`, `friendly-mentor`, `warm`, `encouraging`

### Often confused with

**email**: An email is a business message designed for the inbox scan: the subject line summarizes the point, the body leads with purpose or action, and the context is self-contained. A thank-you note has one subject (a specific act), one direction (toward the recipient), and no asks. When a thank-you note gains an agenda or a second purpose, it has become an email, not a note.
