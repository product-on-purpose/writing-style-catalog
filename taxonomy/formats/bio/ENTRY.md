## Bio

A bio is a short third-person summary of who a person is professionally - their role, notable work, credentials, and the throughline of their career - written to introduce them in a program, byline, panel, or about page. Unlike a list of credentials, a bio is narrative and curated: it selects the details that matter for this reader in this context and arranges them into a brief story that gives the audience what they need to receive the person well.

The bio's defining move is selection. A professional accumulates far more roles, projects, and credentials than any bio can carry; the writer chooses the two or three that explain who this person is in the context at hand. A speaker bio for a technology conference foregrounds different work than a byline for a literary journal - same person, different selections, because the reader arriving at each venue needs different context. The format earns its place by doing this work that a list cannot: a list of all credentials leaves the reader to construct the throughline themselves; a bio hands them the throughline already drawn.

Third person is not convention for its own sake. It signals that someone else is vouching for this person, which is the social function a bio performs. A conference program bio reads as the organization introducing the speaker; a book jacket bio reads as the publisher presenting the author. First person would shift that register from curated introduction to personal statement, which changes how a reader weighs the claims.

### Canonical template

```
[Full name] is [current role or title] at [organization or affiliation]. [He/She/They]
[notable work or defining achievement in one sentence]. [Second notable credential, project,
or career move that completes the throughline - optional for short bios]. [Context-fitting
closer: current project, where based, relevant personal note, or what comes next - optional
and venue-dependent].
```

### When to use

Introducing a speaker, panelist, or guest host in a program, event guide, or on-screen lower-third; writing an author byline for an article, essay, or book jacket; creating an About page or professional profile on a personal site or platform; submitting a contributor profile or media kit one-sheet for a publication or event; providing background on a podcast guest, webinar presenter, or conference keynote.

### When not to use

When an evaluator needs a comprehensive, scannable credentials record to assess hiring fit - use a resume; when the reader must compare qualifications across multiple candidates simultaneously; when the format explicitly calls for structured sections, labeled headers, and parallel evidence.

### Pairs well with

`direct-communicator`, `storyteller`, `confident`, `warm`, `narrative-case-study`

### Often confused with

**resume**: A resume is a structured, scannable record of a person's professional experience, skills, and qualifications, organized for a recruiter to assess fit in seconds. It presents credentials through reverse-chronological roles, bulleted accomplishments, and labeled sections so a screener can locate signal without reading prose - its defining quality is factual density and compression, not narrative. A bio is a flowing paragraph that tells a brief story for a reader who needs context, selecting and framing a handful of details into a throughline rather than inventorying the full record.
