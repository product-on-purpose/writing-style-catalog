## Eulogy

A eulogy is the tribute spoken at a funeral or memorial service that brings a person's life into focus through what they actually did, said, and meant to the people around them. Its purpose is not to summarize a life - that would be an obituary - but to make the person present one more time by recounting something specific: a phrase they always used, a choice they made when no one was watching, the particular way they entered a room. The specific detail does the emotional work that abstract praise cannot.

The format moves through three beats: an opening that grounds the speaker and acknowledges the occasion without dwelling in it, a body that carries the weight of the tribute through two or three selected memories, and a close that names what the person leaves behind without overpromising. The opening does not need a joke or a quote - it needs to land the speaker in the room. The body should hold no more than two or three stories; more dilutes the effect. The close names what remains, not only what was lost.

### Canonical template

```
# [Name]
[Date and occasion]

## Opening
[Ground the speaker in the room; acknowledge the occasion without lingering]

## First memory
[A specific moment, named detail, or story - concrete, not summarized]

## Second memory
[A different facet of the person, grounded in an equally specific moment]

## Bridge (optional)
[How these moments add up to what this person meant]

## Close
[What the person leaves behind; what the room carries forward]
```

### When to use

Honoring someone at a funeral, memorial, or celebration of life; delivering a tribute where the speaker has direct personal knowledge of the person; when the primary goal is to make the person present one more time rather than list biographical facts; when the audience includes people with varying relationships to the deceased.

### When not to use

Biographical summaries or obituaries where factual completeness outweighs emotional resonance; commemorations where the speaker has no personal relationship with the subject; awards or recognition ceremonies where the person is living and the register is celebratory rather than memorial.

### Pairs well with

`storyteller`, `pastoral`, `reverent`, `warm`, `narrative-case-study`

### Often confused with

**devotional-entry**: A devotional entry centers universal spiritual themes - scripture, meditation, and reflection directed toward the reader's own faith or growth. A eulogy centers the specific person being remembered; spiritual content may appear at the edges but the core is always that particular life, not universal truth.
