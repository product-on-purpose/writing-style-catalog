## Gratitude Journal

A gratitude journal entry is a dated, recurring personal practice of naming what the writer is
thankful for - usually a short list or a few sentences, written for the writer alone. Its value
is the discipline of attention: noticing and recording the good. The format asks one question -
what am I grateful for today? - and answers it. That constraint is a feature, not a limitation.
By narrowing the field of inquiry to what is good, the format trains the writer to find it.

The structure is intentionally minimal. A date, a prompt (explicit or implied), and a brief
enumeration or short prose account of gratitude. Length rarely matters; three genuine entries
outperform a page of forced ones. The discipline is not in how much is written but in the act
of sitting down to look for what is worth naming.

Unlike an open journal entry, which follows wherever honest self-inquiry leads and may sit with
difficulty or confusion, a gratitude journal is deliberately bounded and positively framed. It
does not suppress hard emotions - but it does not pursue them either. The writer who arrives at
the page anxious or depleted is not asked to resolve that; they are asked only to find, within
or alongside it, what they are grateful for. That act of finding is the practice.

### Canonical template

```
[Date]

Grateful for:
- [specific person, moment, or detail]
- [specific person, moment, or detail]
- [specific person, moment, or detail]

[Optional: one sentence on why one item stood out today]
```

### When to use

Building a daily or recurring attention practice focused on noticing what is good, anchoring the start or end of a day with a brief, deliberate positive framing, countering a habitual focus on problems, complaints, or what is missing, supporting a personal wellness, mental health, or spiritual practice, giving children or beginners a structured entry point for reflective writing.

### When not to use

When the writer needs to work through a difficult emotion, decision, or conflict without a forced positive frame; when honest, unguarded self-inquiry that may lead to uncomfortable places is the goal; when depth of reflection across a full range of experience is needed, not a bounded positive scan.

### Pairs well with

`caregiver`, `storyteller`, `warm`, `encouraging`

### Often confused with

**journal-entry**: A journal entry is written for an audience of one - the writer - and follows wherever honest self-inquiry leads. It records what happened and, more importantly, what the writer made of it: thinking on the page, unguarded, without the performance that a reader would invite. It may be tentative, circling, or unresolved - that quality is not a defect. A gratitude journal differs by being deliberately bounded: it asks one question (what am I grateful for today?) and answers it with a positively framed list or brief prose. Where a journal entry may sit with difficulty and unresolved questions, a gratitude journal does not pursue them. The constraint is the point.
