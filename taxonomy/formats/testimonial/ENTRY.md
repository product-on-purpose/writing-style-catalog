## Testimonial

A testimonial is a short first-person endorsement in a customer's own voice, vouching for a product, service, or person based on their direct experience. Its function is social proof - a third-party signal that tells a prospective buyer "someone like you tried this and here is what happened." The authority of the testimonial comes entirely from the source: the customer, not the company, is speaking. That displacement of voice is what makes the format work.

Its credibility comes from being the customer's authentic words, not the seller's. Unlike ad copy, which is the seller's own crafted hook written to grab attention and drive a click, a testimonial speaks in the satisfied customer's voice and earns trust precisely because it is not the company talking about itself. The reader mentally attributes the endorsement to a peer, not a vendor, and that attribution is the mechanism by which the format builds belief.

A strong testimonial is specific and brief. It names a concrete situation, the change or result the customer experienced, and usually closes with a direct recommendation. The most useful testimonials are ones a prospective buyer can locate themselves in - they describe a recognizable problem and a believable outcome, not generic satisfaction. Length runs short: 30 to 150 words is the workable range. Longer than that, and the format tips toward a case study.

Testimonials appear across the full spectrum of channels: product and service pages, sales decks, printed brochures, email campaigns, and social posts. They anchor the social proof layer of a purchase journey wherever a buyer's trust needs corroboration from a source outside the brand.

### Canonical template

```
"[Problem or situation the customer faced before the product or service]
 [The choice they made: what they tried]
 [Outcome: the specific result or change they experienced]
 [Recommendation: a direct endorsement in the customer's own words]"
- [Customer Name], [Title or Role], [Company or context]
```

### When to use

Adding social proof to a product or service page where a prospective buyer needs third-party corroboration, representing a new customer segment or use case you want to attract by showing that someone in that situation succeeded, supporting a sales conversation with a peer endorsement that carries more trust than a vendor claim, launching a new product or feature where brand-authored claims need corroboration from a real customer's experience, including in email campaigns where a peer voice lifts credibility before a conversion moment.

### When not to use

When no real customer has given authentic feedback - a fabricated or heavily edited testimonial destroys the trust the format exists to build. Explaining complex technical capabilities that require structured documentation rather than a personal voice. Communicating pricing, terms, or policies where precision and accountability require authoritative brand language, not informal customer endorsement.

### Pairs well with

`storyteller`, `direct-communicator`, `confident`, `warm`, `narrative-case-study`

### Often confused with

**ad-copy**: Ad copy is short promotional text written in the seller's voice, crafted to grab attention and drive a single action under tight character limits. It leads with a headline hook, adds an optional reinforcing line, and closes with a call to action - all written by the company, about the company's offer. A testimonial differs in the most fundamental way: the speaker is the customer, not the brand. Ad copy is what the company writes about itself to earn a click; a testimonial is what a customer says about the company to earn a prospective buyer's trust.
