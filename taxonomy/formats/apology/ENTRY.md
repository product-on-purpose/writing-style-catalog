## Apology

An apology is a message that takes responsibility for a specific wrong, names its impact on the person harmed, and commits to repair - without excuses or conditions. Its power is in owning the fault plainly. The three components are not interchangeable: name what you did, name what it cost the other person, and commit to a specific repair. An apology that expresses remorse without naming the wrong is incomplete. An apology that names the wrong without naming the impact keeps the writer at the center. An apology that offers no repair is a confession, not a commitment.

What makes an apology fail is almost always what it adds rather than what it omits. Conditional language ("I am sorry if you felt hurt") relocates the harm from the writer's action to the recipient's sensitivity - which is a defense, not an apology. Mitigating context ("I was under a lot of pressure at the time") invites the reader to weigh circumstances against the wrong and divide the accountability. Neither move is an apology; both are defenses in the apology's grammatical form. The test is whether ownership is plain and unconditional: "I did X, it caused Y, I will do Z."

Unlike a thank-you note, which names a specific thing the recipient did and offers gratitude for it, an apology names a specific thing the writer did wrong and offers accountability for it. The structure is symmetrical: both address one specific act, both target a particular person, both stay short and purposeful. But the direction of moral weight is reversed. A thank-you note moves toward what the recipient gave; an apology moves toward what the writer owes.

### Canonical template

```
[Salutation - name the person]

[Name the specific wrong you committed - one direct sentence, no qualifications]

[Name the impact on them - what it cost, hurt, or disrupted for them]

[Commit to repair - a specific behavior change or concrete next step]

[Closing - brief, no request for forgiveness]
```

### When to use

When you have caused specific, nameable harm to another person and need to take responsibility for it, after missing a commitment or breaking trust in a way that damaged a relationship, when acknowledgment of the impact matters more to repair than explanation of the circumstances, when repair requires the other person to know you understand what you did and what it cost them, before or alongside a concrete corrective action so the action has meaningful context.

### When not to use

When you cannot name a specific wrong, when the goal is to negotiate or explain rather than to own, when the wrong is organizational or systemic and no single author can truthfully own it as a personal act.

### Pairs well with

`caregiver`, `direct-communicator`, `candid`, `empathetic`, `problem-solution`

### Often confused with

**thank-you-note**: A thank-you note is a short, specific note of gratitude focused on one person and one specific thing they did. It names the recipient's action and what it made possible, then closes warmly with no ask. An apology has the same brevity and focus on a single act, but the moral direction is reversed: where a thank-you note names what the recipient did and offers gratitude for it, an apology names what the writer did wrong and offers accountability for it.
