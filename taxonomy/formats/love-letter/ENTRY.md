## Love Letter

A love letter is an intimate written expression of romantic affection, addressed to the beloved and dwelling on what they mean to the writer - feelings, memories, longing, devotion. It is personal, unhurried, and meant for an audience of one. Unlike a thank-you note, which names a specific thing the recipient did and offers gratitude for it in a few lines, a love letter is not transactional or occasion-bound: it expresses an enduring feeling rather than acknowledging a discrete act, and its length and intimacy come from dwelling in affection rather than naming a favor.

The form has almost no structural obligations. It may open mid-thought, meander through memory, return to a moment, or sustain a single image across several paragraphs. What holds it together is not structure but devotion: the beloved fills every sentence. Chronology is optional; an argument is unnecessary; a resolution is not required.

Its register is confessional in the best sense - the writer says things they might struggle to say aloud. The letter creates a space for honesty that conversation often cannot hold. Distance, permanence, and the slowness of writing all make it possible to be more truthful than speech allows.

A love letter is not a short form. Length is earned by staying in the feeling rather than adding items to a list. When it is working, the reader feels accompanied. When it is not, it reads like a catalog of praise.

### Canonical template

```
[Salutation - beloved's name or an intimate address]

[Opening - arrive in feeling immediately, or name a specific moment that is on your mind]

[Body - dwell here: a memory, what they mean to you, something about them you keep returning to;
no transitions or argument required, just sustained attention]

[Return - circle back to the feeling you opened with, or name where the feeling rests right now]

[Closing - intimate, no agenda, no call to action]
```

### When to use

Writing to a romantic partner to express how you feel when words in person are not enough, marking a milestone - an anniversary, a reunion, a long separation - with something that will last, saying something difficult or tender that conversation has not made room for, when you want your feelings to exist as an object the person can return to, when the love itself is the subject and not any single act or occasion.

### When not to use

When you need to address a specific grievance or relational problem - the love letter is not a conflict resolution tool, when the relationship is professional or the emotional register would be unwelcome, when time pressure demands a short clear message - a love letter cannot be rushed without becoming hollow.

### Pairs well with

`storyteller`, `caregiver`, `warm`, `confessional`, `chronological-narrative`

### Often confused with

**thank-you-note**: A thank-you note names a specific thing the recipient did, names what it made possible, and stays short - typically 50 to 150 words - with a single subject and no asks. Its power comes from the particular: the discrete act, the concrete impact. A love letter is not occasion-bound or act-specific: it expresses an enduring feeling rather than acknowledging something the beloved did, and it earns its length by dwelling in affection rather than precision. When a love letter pivots to thanking the beloved for a specific gesture, it has crossed into the territory of the thank-you note.
