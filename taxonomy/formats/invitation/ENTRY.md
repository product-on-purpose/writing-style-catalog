## Invitation

An invitation is a message that asks someone to attend an event and gives them what they need to decide and respond. The essential elements are what the occasion is, when and where it will happen, and how the reader can say yes - or no. Beyond the logistics, an invitation carries the tone of the occasion itself: a formal gala reads differently from a neighborhood cookout, and both read differently from a child's birthday party. The writer's first job is to match the register to the event, so the reader arrives knowing not just the details but how to prepare, what to expect, and how much it matters.

What makes the invitation distinct is the guest relationship. The invitation is addressed to a specific person or defined circle of people who are being welcomed - not informed as members of an audience, but invited as guests whose presence is wanted and whose answer is expected. The RSVP is not a courtesy add-on; it is structural. Without a reply mechanism, the message may have the content of an invitation but not its function, because an invitation is the opening move of a two-way exchange.

The invitation differs from an announcement, which tells an audience about news and expects no reply. An announcement informs people about something new or changing and closes with a next step for the reader; it is one-way, addressed to an audience rather than to a guest. An invitation requests the reader's presence and waits for a response. Writing an invitation as a broadcast - no direct address to the guest, no RSVP, no sense that the reader's answer is wanted - removes the very thing that makes it an invitation.

### Canonical template

```
[Occasion / event title - what this is]

[You are invited to... / Please join us for...] [brief description of the event]

When: [date and time]
Where: [location or link]
[Dress / what to bring / special note (optional)]

RSVP: [by date, via method]
```

### When to use

Asking specific people to attend a celebratory occasion - a wedding, birthday, graduation, anniversary, or holiday gathering. Inviting someone to a professional or community event where their presence is requested and a reply is needed. Welcoming a guest to a smaller gathering where knowing who will attend affects planning or arrangements. Requesting attendance at any occasion where the reader should feel personally welcomed rather than publicly notified. Any situation where the writer needs to convey the formality, festivity, or warmth of an upcoming occasion before the event itself.

### When not to use

When publicizing an open event that anyone may attend without a specific guest list or reply needed - use an announcement instead. When delivering news or information about an event without asking for the reader's attendance or response. When the communication is an ongoing exchange or conversation rather than a single, complete attendance request.

### Pairs well with

`friendly-mentor`, `caregiver`, `warm`, `celebratory`, `playful`

### Often confused with

**announcement**: An announcement is a direct message that tells an audience about something new or changing, in the organization's own voice. It leads with the news, explains what the change means for the reader, and closes with a next step; it is one-way and addressed to an audience rather than to a guest. No reply is expected or requested. An invitation, by contrast, is addressed to a specific guest whose presence is wanted and whose reply is expected; it opens a two-way exchange rather than delivering one-way news.
