The first Sunday I actually stopped, I sat on the back steps for twenty minutes staring at the fence.

Not thinking. Not planning. Just sitting. My phone was inside, face-down on the kitchen counter, and I kept reaching for the pocket where it usually was and finding nothing.

My neighbor Marcus came out to water his tomatoes. He waved. I waved back. He didn't say anything and neither did I. He turned the hose on and the smell of wet earth came over the fence.

I had tried this before. I had tried it maybe a dozen times. Each time I had framed it differently in my journal - sabbath, recovery day, intentional downtime - as if the right word would make it feel like something other than losing ground. By noon I would find myself at my desk with a browser open, telling myself I was just checking one thing.

This time I told my wife Clara I was going to try again. She looked up from her book. "You want me to hide the laptop?"

"Maybe," I said.

She didn't. But I left it in the car.

The afternoon was slow in a way that felt wrong at first and then didn't. I read a chapter of a novel I'd been carrying around for six weeks. I ate lunch without a podcast. Clara and I sat on the porch until it got cold and then we moved inside and she made tea and I watched her make it - the specific domestic ritual of it, the way she always sets the timer for exactly four minutes.

I didn't solve any problems that day. I didn't catch up on anything.

But Tuesday I sat down at my desk and something was different. Not dramatic. Just - I could see what mattered and what didn't, in a way I usually couldn't until Friday. Like the week had been given a different shape than the one I'd put it in.

I'm not sure what rest costs. I thought I knew - the output, the edge, the hours. But I'm less sure now what I was buying with them.
