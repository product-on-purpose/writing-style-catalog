# What the Day Costs

It was a Saturday in November when I first tried to leave my phone in the kitchen. I made it forty minutes before I walked back in, just to check. I told myself it was about the weather.

> "And he said to them, 'Come away by yourselves to a desolate place and rest a while.' For many were coming and going, and they had no leisure even to eat." - Mark 6:31

The disciples hadn't been idle. They had just returned from being sent out, healing, preaching, doing the exact work they were called to do. And Jesus called them away anyway. Not as a reward for finishing, not as a break before the next assignment, but as the thing itself. Rest. Come away.

I used to read that word "away" and feel only the warmth of the invitation. It took years before I felt its edge. Away from the inbox. Away from the half-finished draft sitting open on my desk like a sentence I hadn't completed. Away from the low, ambient hum of production that I had learned to mistake for purpose.

The first few times I tried to keep a real day of rest, an actual deliberate stopping rather than just a slow afternoon, I felt something close to guilt. Not the loud, accusatory kind. The background kind. The kind that whispers you are falling behind, that others are not stopping, that this will cost you. I thought I was failing at rest because I found it so uncomfortable. I didn't understand yet that the discomfort is part of what the practice is asking. You cannot be called away and feel nothing about what you are leaving.

What I found, slowly and with some surprise, was that the day did not simply vanish. It did something stranger: it reordered the days around it. The Thursday before began to take on a shape. The Monday after arrived with something steadier in it. I could not have told you why at first, and I still cannot fully explain it. The clarity I had been chasing through longer hours was often waiting for me on the other side of stopping.

The pull to check one more thing has not disappeared. I still walk back to the kitchen sometimes. But I have started to notice the pull itself as a kind of question, not whether to give in, but what I am trusting when I do. If I cannot set the work down for a single day, what does that say about what I believe the work rests on?

The Sabbath is not a productivity strategy. It is a practice of trust. It makes the same claim the disciples heard on that crowded afternoon: the world does not need my watching it for twenty-four hours. That is harder to believe than it sounds.

---

Set this down for a moment. Not to make a better plan for resting next week. Just to sit with the question: what would it mean, this week, to stop - and to believe that stopping is enough?

---

*Lord, teach me to rest as you rested, not from emptiness but from completion. Help me trust that what I set down is held by hands more steady than mine. Quiet the voice in me that calls busyness faithfulness. Let this day be holy not because I earned it, but because you named it so. Amen.*
