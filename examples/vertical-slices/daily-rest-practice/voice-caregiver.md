The first thing I noticed was how much my hands wanted to reach for the phone.

Not because anything urgent was happening. Just because they knew where it was. The chat tool had a red dot. There were probably emails. The ticket tracker does not empty itself.

That first rest day, I lasted about forty minutes before I picked up the phone and read eleven messages that could have waited.

This is the difficulty: the body does not know it is allowed to stop. You have trained it for years to stay alert, to track what is open, to feel a small pull of guilt at stillness. That training does not pause because you decided to rest. It keeps firing. You feel the absence of output like something left undone.

I am not going to tell you the feeling goes away quickly. It does not. The first few Sundays were hard. Restless. A low anxiety that I could not name. The body does not understand that lying on the couch is the work now.

What I can tell you is what actually happened, slowly.

The week started to separate itself. There was a seam in it, and on one side was the doing, and on the other side was the putting down. I began to sleep differently in the second half of those weeks. I noticed what I was hungry for. I had a conversation with my daughter where I was actually there, not half-managing whatever was open in my head.

You do not have to believe this will work before you try it. You do not have to feel rested on the first day. You are allowed to feel strange, and useless, and a little anxious.

The rest does not ask you to feel good about it. It only asks you to stop.

That much you can do. Put the phone in the other room. Eat something. Sit somewhere soft.

The week will wait. It always has.
