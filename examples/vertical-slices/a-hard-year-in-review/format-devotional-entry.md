The final email came in late December, timestamped 11:47 p.m. I read it twice, then put my phone face-down on the desk and sat with the dark window for a while. That was how the Millbrook project ended.

---

> "Remember my affliction and my wandering, the wormwood and the gall. My soul continually remembers it and is bowed down within me."
> - Lamentations 3:19-20

---

The writer of Lamentations is not trying to get somewhere. This is not a psalm that pivots. The famous lines three stanzas later - the ones about mercies being new every morning - do appear, and they are real. But the writer earns them by first refusing to rush past the wreckage. Wormwood is bitter root. Gall is a bile-flavored grief. The writer names both, with something close to precision.

This year I needed that permission. The Millbrook partnership had consumed most of three years. I believed in what we were building, and I brought other people in with me - people who trusted my read of the situation. When the funding fell through and the core team dispersed in March, I kept expecting a clean feeling to arrive: relief, or at least a settled sadness I could file away. It did not arrive. What arrived instead was the recurring memory of a particular Tuesday planning session - all that energy, the whiteboard covered in markers, the room loud with possibility. That image still shows up when I am trying to sleep.

And then, separately, there was the friendship. Claire and I had been close for six years. I will not narrate the whole of it here because it is not only my story to tell, and because I am still not sure what I got wrong and what I only got wrong in her accounting. What I know is that by summer we had stopped calling, and by fall the silence had the weight of a decision neither of us officially made.

I am telling you both of these things because I want to be honest: they did not teach me the same lesson. The project ending and the friendship changing are not secretly the same grief wearing two coats. I got some things wrong in the first and probably different things wrong in the second, and the year asked me to hold them separately even when I wanted to collapse them into one clean story I could understand.

What I am taking into the new year is not a lesson, exactly. It is more like a practice. When I catch myself reaching for a frame - "it was a season of growth," "everything happens for a reason," the usual equipment we hand each other - I am trying to set the frame down and just stay with what happened. The Millbrook project ended. The friendship changed. My soul is still bowed down in some places. That is not a stopping point; it is a starting point. Honest accounting is not the same as despair.

There is one thing I am choosing to carry: the Tuesday session, the whiteboard, the energy in the room. Not as a reminder of what I lost, but as evidence of what I was capable of believing in. The believing is mine, even when the outcome is not.

---

What in this past year are you still holding at arm's length because naming it fully would make it too real? What would it mean to set down the frame and just let it be what it was?

---

*Lord, I am not asking you to make this year make sense. I am asking for the honesty to look at it clearly - to see what I got wrong without collapsing into it, and to see what was genuinely lost without having to call it secretly good. Keep me from the stories that comfort me too easily. And stay close to the places that are still bowed down.*
