# The Async-First Manifesto

*Why we killed the daily standup, and what we're committing to instead.*

This team ran on one assumption nobody ever wrote down: if something mattered, we said it out loud, together, at the same time. That worked when we were six engineers close enough in time zones to share a morning. It stopped working somewhere in the eighteen months it took us to grow into eleven engineers spread across Pacific, Eastern, the UK, and India, and we kept holding the same 9am Pacific standup anyway - a meeting that lands at 9:30pm for the three engineers who joined us from India. Fourteen minutes a day, and by our own count, about four of those minutes were worth anyone's time. The rest was status spoken out loud and gone, with no record anyone could search the next time the same problem came back.

Nobody chose this on purpose. We just never stopped to ask who was still awake for it. We are stopping now.

## What We Believe

**Written is the default, not the fallback.** If something is worth saying, it is worth writing down. A status update spoken once and never repeated is not a record, it is a rumor. We choose text first: searchable, re-readable, and there for whoever needs it at 6am or 11pm.

**Every time zone is a home time zone.** No clock on this team owns the calendar by default. Pacific is not the anchor just because it was convenient back when we were smaller. We design our rituals around the whole team's day, not around whichever quarter of it is easiest to schedule.

**Presence is not the same as work.** An engineer who posts a clear, honest update from India before their own 10am cutoff has participated. An engineer who sits silent through a live call at 9am Pacific because the culture expects a body in the square has not, no matter how it looks on a calendar.

**Unwritten knowledge gets solved twice.** We watched three separate cases last quarter where someone burned over an hour rediscovering a bug a teammate had already found and mentioned once, out loud, in a meeting nobody could search afterward. That is not the cost of moving fast. It is the cost of moving carelessly.

**Real-time meetings are for judgment, not status.** We are not against meetings. We are against meetings that could have been three sentences in a channel. When we do get on a call, it is because something needs a live back-and-forth: a decision, a disagreement, a design that will not survive being flattened into bullet points.

## What We're Done With

We are done with the standup as theater: a status round-robin performed for the benefit of whoever is in the room, timed to the convenience of headquarters, forgotten the moment the call ends. We are done treating a person's silence during their own middle of the night as a discipline problem instead of a design flaw. We are done measuring engagement by who was awake to watch a meeting instead of by what they shipped.

## What We're Choosing Instead

We choose a written update, every weekday, in #team-standup, from every engineer, posted in their own morning. We choose blockers that get a name and an @mention instead of a shrug in a room half the team never heard. We choose one protected weekly session for the conversations that actually need two people talking at once, and we choose to cancel it without guilt when nothing needs it. We choose to trust each other's word in writing as much as we ever trusted a voice on a call.

## Starting Today

Every one of us posts by 10am in our own local time: what shipped, what's in progress, what's blocked and who can unblock it. Whoever is on call reads the channel and chases every open blocker like it is theirs, because for that day it is. Thursday is protected for whatever actually needs a live room; if nothing does, we cancel by Wednesday afternoon instead of meeting out of habit.

We are giving this thirty days, not because we need more proof that the old way asked our India-based teammates to sit through a status meeting at 9:30pm, night after night, but because thirty days is what it takes for a new habit to feel normal instead of forced. If this is wrong, say so where the whole team can see it, not in a side channel that only reaches whoever shares your time zone. But show up for the thirty days first. Belief without practice is just a nicer-sounding meeting.

This is not a pilot we are quietly hoping fails so we can slide back to what was comfortable for whoever was already in the room. Async-first is not a trial we are running. It is who we are now.

*Posted to the team wiki by the engineering manager, on behalf of a team that finally gets to start its own day on its own clock.*
