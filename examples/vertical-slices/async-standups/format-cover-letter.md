Dear Hiring Team,

I'm writing to apply for the Director of Engineering role at Alderwood Systems. Your posting asks for someone who can keep a fast-growing, multi-timezone organization coordinated without letting process outgrow the team, and that is the exact problem I solved as engineering manager for an 11-person team spread across four time zones at Fernbridge Software.

When that team grew from 6 to 11 engineers over 18 months, our daily 9am Pacific standup stopped working: it landed at 9:30pm for our India-based engineers, whose attendance had fallen to 3.2 of 5 sessions a week against 4.6 for our US-based engineers, and the meeting itself produced about 4 minutes of decision-relevant content inside every 14-minute call. I replaced it with a three-field async update posted to a shared channel by 10am local time, backed by a single weekly working session held only when something actually needed real-time discussion. Inside the first two weeks, on-time posting rose from 78 percent to 85.5 percent, the median time from a flagged blocker to a substantive reply fell to 18 minutes, and our India-based engineers posted every weekday for the first time in the team's history.

The role also calls for practices that outlast any one manager's attention. I wrote the change up as a formal ADR and a process README specifically so a new hire could run the format without me in the room, and the roughly 70 minutes per engineer per week it recovered is the kind of number that only holds up if the system keeps working after I stop watching it. That is the habit I would bring to setting direction across several teams instead of one.

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how this experience applies to the Director of Engineering role in an interview this week.
