The morning meeting ends and Marcus stays on the call just a little longer than everyone else. You have noticed this before. He mutes himself during the breakout, waits for the sidebar chatter to quiet, and then asks the question he could not ask in front of the team: "Are we making a mistake going back?" You know this feeling - the uncertainty that hides behind a policy question.

This is where we are. Our organization is deciding how we work together, and the question has grown bigger than desks and commutes. It is a question about belonging, about what we owe each other, and about what it means to be a community at all.

In Ecclesiastes 4, the Preacher makes a blunt case: "Two are better than one, because they have a good return for their labor." He is not romantic about togetherness. He names the immediate, practical reason for it - the one who falls can be lifted. The cord of three strands holds. But notice what he does not say. He does not say you must always be in the same room.

This matters because the gift of presence is not the same as the mandate of proximity. When your team gathers around the same table on shared anchor days, something real happens. Trust builds in the gaps between agenda items, in the conversation after the meeting ends. You know this. And you also know that the colleague who joined your team from three states away brought something you would not have found downtown.

The hard truth is this: you are not choosing between community and flexibility. You are choosing how to steward both.

A few shared days, held with intention. The rest returned to the people doing the work.

This is difficult. It asks something real of everyone - office-first leaders who have seen collaboration erode, and remote advocates who have watched their best hours reclaimed. It does not promise comfort to either side. It offers instead a harder thing: shared accountability for a community that is genuinely worth gathering for.
