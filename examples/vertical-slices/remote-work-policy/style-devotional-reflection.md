The last time I saw Marcus was in a parking lot. We had both walked out of the building at the same time, and we stood there for a few minutes, neither of us quite ready to go. He mentioned something offhand about a project, I said something back, and somewhere in that conversation - I still cannot say exactly when - we solved a problem that had been sitting in the ticket tracker for three weeks.

We would not have done that on a video call. Not because video calls are inferior. But because we were not trying to solve anything. We were just standing there, in the late afternoon, letting the day unspool.

I have been turning that moment over since the return-to-office debates began in earnest at our company. The office-first voices say it plainly: you cannot manufacture serendipity. The fully-remote voices say it just as plainly: you cannot manufacture focus in an open floor plan, and you cannot hire Marcus at all if he lives in a city where you have no office.

Both of these things are true. I have felt the truth of both on the same afternoon.

What I am less sure of is whether we are asking the right question when we ask "office or not." The choice presented as binary carries a hidden assumption: that one default fits the work, the worker, and the relationship at the same time. It rarely does.

There was a retreat I attended years ago where the leader said something I have never quite shaken: "Community is not the same as proximity. But proximity is one of the few environments where community has room to surprise you." She was talking about something else entirely. But I find myself returning to it when I sit in this debate.

The hybrid model I am asking us to consider is not a compromise in the sense of everyone getting less than they wanted. It is closer to a rhythm - a few shared anchor days where we can be surprised by a parking lot conversation, and the rest of the week given back to the kind of focused work that asks the world to leave you alone for a while.

The office-first leaders will hear this as surrender. The fully-remote advocates will hear it as encroachment. I understand both readings. But I wonder if what we are really negotiating is not a policy at all. We are negotiating what kind of organization we want to be, and what kinds of moments we are willing to protect.

What would it mean for us to choose a rhythm rather than a rule?
