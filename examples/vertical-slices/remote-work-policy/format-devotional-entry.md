The moment lasted four seconds, and without it, we would not have solved what we were trying to solve that afternoon.

*"Two are better than one, because they have a good return for their labor: If either of them falls down, one can help the other up. But pity anyone who falls and has no one to help them up. Also, if two lie down together, they will keep warm. But how can one keep warm alone? Though one may be overpowered, two can defend themselves. A cord of three strands is not quickly broken."*

Ecclesiastes 4:9-12

---

This passage has nothing to do with office layouts. It was written for a world without offices. And yet it names something I have been turning over as our organization debates whether to bring people back, send them home, or land somewhere in between.

The preacher in Ecclesiastes is not arguing that people should never work alone. He is observing that isolation, over time, creates a kind of cold. Not dramatic failure - just the slow chill of working without someone to fall beside you or pull you back up. The warmth he describes is not productivity. It is the harder-to-measure thing: being known, being covered, being held to account in a way that feels like care rather than surveillance.

I have watched this dynamic play out on both sides of the hybrid debate. The colleague who chose full remote found genuine focus she had lost to open-plan noise. She also told me, quietly, that she had stopped knowing what her teammates were actually working on, and that she no longer got the off-the-cuff edits that used to sharpen her thinking. The colleague who returned five days a week said the energy had come back, and also that his commute was costing him two hours a day that used to go to reading and rest.

Neither was wrong.

What Ecclesiastes does not give us is a scheduling solution. It gives us a value: the cord of three strands holds because of what is woven into it, not simply because the strands happen to be adjacent. The question our policy debate keeps skating past is not "how often?" but "what are we trying to weave?"

If I am honest, I believe we need enough shared time to build the kind of trust that lets someone hand you a marker without making it a transaction. A few anchor days each week - chosen with intention, not mandated as theater - and then the rest of the time in whatever conditions help each person think clearly. Not a perfect answer. But closer to what this passage is describing than either extreme on its own.

---

Think about the last time a colleague helped you in a way so small they probably forgot it happened. Where were you?

---

*Lord, keep us from mistaking proximity for community and flexibility for isolation. Give us the wisdom to see what we are actually building when we make these policies, and whether we are building it for the people who need covering. Help us hold this together as a cord, not as a collection of strands. Amen.*
