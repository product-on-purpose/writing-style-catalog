# Where Work Happens: A Declaration

*A declaration from the Work-Location Policy Working Group, published for our own people and for anyone outside this company still relitigating the same fight.*

Every return-to-office debate happening right now is being run as a war with exactly two acceptable outcomes: everyone back in a chair five days a week, or nobody ever required to share a room again. We reject the premise. A workplace is not obligated to choose between control and abdication, and we are done pretending those are the only two shapes a policy can take.

We spent two quarters inside this argument before we understood what we actually believed: not a compromise position, not a survey of what each camp would tolerate, but a worldview about what presence is for and what it is not for. We are ready to state it plainly and hold to it.

**We believe:**

- Presence is a tool for coordination, not a proxy for commitment. A chair filled five days a week proves nothing about the work done in it.
- Flexibility without structure is not trust. It is abdication wearing trust's clothes, and the people who built their lives on top of it deserve better than a placeholder nobody actually decided.
- Talent has no commute radius. Every policy that pretends otherwise is a talent policy in denial.
- A commitment made during hiring is a commitment, not a draft. We do not get to discover, a year later, that "flexible" meant "for now."
- Trust is built in scheduled proximity, not enforced by constant proximity. A room shared on purpose, on a known schedule, builds more of it than a room that is either always full or never full.
- A policy that hedges to keep every camp comfortable ends up defending nothing. We would rather be argued with than be vague.

**What we stand against.** We stand against the binary itself, the idea that the only honest positions are full mandate or full abdication. We stand against "flexible" used as a placeholder for a decision nobody actually made, the arrangement that let ambiguity harden into policy by default while two groups of real people built incompatible expectations on top of it. And we stand against calling an honest middle ground a compromise, as though landing somewhere other than an extreme were a failure of nerve instead of a decision made on purpose.

**What we stand for.** Two anchor days, Tuesday and Thursday, when everyone who can reach a room is in one. Three days that are actually flexible, not flexible with an asterisk: no approval required, no case made for permission. A line held for a full quarter before anyone is allowed to renegotiate it, because a policy that bends in its first month was never a policy. Presence used to coordinate, never to surveil.

Here is what we intend to do. We will hold Tuesday and Thursday the way we said we would, not the way "flexible" quietly stopped meaning flexible. We will build the mentorship structure new hires need to learn this place in two shared days a week, because we are not calling a known gap solved before we have closed it. And we will publish our reasoning, not just our conclusion, so that other organizations relitigating this same fight do not have to start from an empty page.

Here is what we ask of anyone who stands with us. Protect the anchor days from becoming optional, one quiet exception at a time, the way the last policy drifted into nothing. Show up on the two days that matter so the other three can stay genuinely, defensibly yours. And if you are a leader still choosing between the two extremes on offer: stop. There is a third option, designed on purpose instead of inherited by accident. We are living proof it holds.

We did not split the difference. We built something else entirely, and we are done being quiet about it.
