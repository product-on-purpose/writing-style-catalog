There is something that happens when people share a room for long enough - not the meeting, not the agenda item that prompted it, but something quieter and harder to name. A colleague makes a small joke at the margin of a conversation, and you learn something about how they think. Someone hesitates before answering a question, and you understand the weight they are carrying. These moments do not appear in any ticket tracker. They are the kind of knowing that does not transfer through a screen.

And yet. The hours a person surrenders to commuting are not abstract. They are hours that belong to someone - hours that might have been given to a child, to focused work, to the long quiet that the open-plan office prevents. To require the commute without reason is to take something that was never ours to take.

This is why a deliberate hybrid deserves careful attention - not the hybrid that permits remote work as a concession, but the hybrid designed around what genuinely requires presence: a few shared anchor days for the work of building trust before a difficult project, for the early alignment that prevents ten confused weeks downstream. The rest of the time returned to the people who will do the deeper work.

The fully-remote advocate will say that presence is overrated, and there is truth in that. The office-first leader will say that remote work erodes culture, and there is truth in that too. What neither honors is what the other values. A policy designed with care for both - for the relationship and for the person who holds it - has to hold both truths at once, without dismissing either, without pretending the tension is not real.
