The morning Marcus finally came back to the office, he brought a lemon pound cake and set it on the counter by the coffee machine. Nobody had told him to. He had not seen the inside of that kitchen in fourteen months.

By ten o'clock, four conversations had happened around that cake that would never have happened over the chat tool. Two of them were about work. One of them changed something.

That afternoon, a VP named Sandra sent the all-hands message: full return to office, five days, same as before. The thread hit two hundred replies in an hour. A designer named Priya, who had moved her family three states away the previous spring, wrote: "I'd have to choose between this job and my daughter's school."

Sandra believed she was solving a culture problem. She was probably right about the problem. But the cure was asking some people to give up something real - time, geography, family proximity - in exchange for a benefit that would never show up credited directly to them.

The counter-argument ran its own risks. Raquel, who managed a team of six across four time zones, admitted that two of her people had never met each other in person. "We ship fine," she said, on the call where her manager pressed her on it. "I just don't know if we trust each other yet."

That is the thing nobody says plainly. Trust is not built in the ticket tracker. It is built in the in-between moments - the hallway, the slightly-too-long lunch, the time someone saw you upset and pretended not to, and that mattered.

Three anchor days. Not five. Not zero. Enough to let the pound cake do its quiet work, enough to leave Priya her life.
