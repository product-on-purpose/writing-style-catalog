I want you to get something real out of this conversation, not just my opinion wrapped in policy language.

You are navigating something genuinely hard. If you lead a team that thrives on in-person rhythm, you are not wrong that offices build things video calls cannot. If you have built your life around remote work, you are not wrong that the commute hours you reclaimed belong to you. I am not here to dismiss your situation.

Here is where I land: a deliberate hybrid, with a few shared anchor days and the rest flexible. Not as a compromise that pleases no one, but as a structure worth defending on its own terms.

The case for anchor days is not that offices are better. It is that some kinds of trust take a room. When you have worked alongside someone through a hard conversation, or eaten lunch while the project was still uncertain, you carry that into the months of async work that follow. That is worth protecting, and you cannot schedule it in the abstract.

But the rest of your week - the focused work, the deep thinking, your personal rhythms - those belong to you. Forcing that work into an office does not improve it. It costs you time, and it costs us the talent we cannot reach when geography is the filter.

I know this does not satisfy everyone here. Office-first colleagues, I hear that you want the whole team present. Remote advocates, I hear that anchor days feel like the beginning of a slow rollback. Both of you deserve a real answer, not reassurance.

What we owe each other is to design those anchor days with genuine intention - and then to trust you with the rest.
