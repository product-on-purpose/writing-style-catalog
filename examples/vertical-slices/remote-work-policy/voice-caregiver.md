The commute is real. Two hours, some days. The chair that was empty at dinner more nights than you want to say. The coffee drunk cold because something always came up before you could sit down.

That is what I want to name before anything else.

Both sides of this conversation have good reasons. The leaders who want everyone back in person are not wrong about what shared space does. Trust grows in the hallway. It grows in the pause before the meeting starts, in the lunch no one planned, in the moment someone sees something on your face and asks if you have a minute. That is real. The people who want to stay fully remote are not wrong either. The hour returned to their morning. The focused afternoon without the noise of an open floor. The colleague they could hire three states away. Also real.

We are not being asked to choose between two bad options. But I want to say carefully: this hybrid proposal asks something of people who have already rearranged their lives. I know that. You organized your mornings around the home desk, or you arranged your childcare around a certain schedule, and now we are asking you to reorganize again. That is not a small thing, and it would be dishonest to call it easy.

Here is what I am actually asking for. Two or three anchor days each week, shared by the whole team, so that when you come in, there is someone to come in to. The rest of the time is yours to arrange. It is not a return to five days. It is smaller than that, and more honest about why we need the room.

This part will be an adjustment. I am not going to tell you it will not be. What I am asking is that we try it before deciding it cannot work, and that we pay attention to who it is hardest for.
