The whiteboard in their conference room had three colors of sticky notes - yellow for what was working, pink for what was broken, green for what customers said they wanted next. By Friday afternoon, nobody could agree on which pile mattered most. Three people had three different stories about what the customers had said, and the roadmap for the next quarter was a guess dressed up in confidence it had not earned.

I have seen that room. I suspect you have too.

> Write the vision; make it plain on tablets, so he may run who reads it.
> - Habakkuk 2:2

Habakkuk received this word in a season of straining to see. The people around him could not make sense of what was happening, and the prophet himself had been asking hard questions. The instruction that came back was not "gather more data" or "hold another meeting." It was simpler and more demanding: write it plain, not for yourself, but for the person who will have to carry it while running.

That word "run" carries something. A vision written for a runner cannot hide in complexity. It cannot live only in the head of the one who received it. It has to be transferable - clear enough that someone can pick it up in motion and know exactly where they are going.

Most teams are not short of feedback. Customers have said things. They have emailed and called and filled out the form and mentioned it after the demo. The failure is rarely the listening. The failure comes at the next step, where someone has to take all of that scattered voice and write it plain enough that the team can run. That step asks for a kind of ruthlessness about what actually matters and a kind of generosity about making it visible to everyone.

This is the problem that Tidemark was built for. Tidemark is a new tool that helps small teams gather customer feedback from wherever it lives - the support queue, the chat channel, the interview notes - and turn it into a single ranked roadmap the whole team can see and share. It launches next week.

The assumption behind it is the same one Habakkuk carried: your people have already written the vision. Your customers have already told you what is working, what is missing, and what would change things for them. What most teams are short of is not input. It is the writing-plain step. The moment where the scattered voice becomes something others can run with.

If your team is sitting in front of a pile of feedback and not sure what belongs on the roadmap next, Tidemark may be worth a look. You can get early access at tidemark.io when doors open next week.

But sit with the image for a moment before you do anything else. Write the vision, and make it plain. Not as a productivity strategy. Because the people running with your work deserve to know where they are going.

*Lord, give us ears to hear what the people we serve are actually telling us. Help us resist the temptation to keep the vision inside our heads, where it cannot be carried by anyone else. Teach us to write it plain - generously and clearly - so that others can run. May the work of our hands be something worth following. Amen.*
