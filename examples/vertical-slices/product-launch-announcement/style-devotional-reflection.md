There is a particular kind of stillness that settles after a long sprint review. The retro is done. The tickets are closed. And then someone asks, quietly, what should we work on next - and the room goes soft with that question.

You have been here. You know the way the roadmap decision feels in that moment: like reaching for something solid and finding instead a collection of signals. The feedback that arrived through the chat tool, three weeks ago. The complaints scattered through the support queue. The note in the shared spreadsheet that someone marked "to review" and then never quite returned to. Each one partial. None of them arranged into anything you could hold up and say with confidence: this is what we heard, and this is what it means.

What you are sitting with in that moment is not only a process problem. It is something closer to a question of faithfulness. Your customers have spent the small currency of their attention on typing out what matters to them. They have trusted that you were listening. The scattered state of the feedback is a kind of debt - not a moral failure, but an incompleteness. You gathered the signal. You have not yet honored it by making it coherent.

This is the moment Tidemark was made for.

It gathers the feedback from wherever it already lives - the queue, the spreadsheet, the notes from conversations - and surfaces what rises to the top across all of it. It produces a ranked, shareable roadmap: not a project for next quarter, but something you could put in front of a stakeholder, or a customer, today. It is not a replacement for judgment. It is a structure that makes sure the signal does not stay silent just because it arrived in pieces, on different days, through different channels.

Tidemark opens to anyone next week. If you have been sitting with that quiet question at the end of your sprints - the one that keeps returning, the one your team already knows how to ask - you are the person it was built for.

The question is a good one. It means you are listening. The next question is: what would it mean to answer it?
