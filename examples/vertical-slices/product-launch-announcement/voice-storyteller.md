Priya opened three browser tabs before her coffee was poured.

There was the spreadsheet - forty-seven rows of customer quotes she had typed from sales calls over six weeks. There was the chat tool, where someone from engineering had pasted a different set of complaints on a Tuesday two months ago, in a channel she could never quite remember. There was the ticket tracker, with sixty-three open items, some tagged "feedback" and most not.

The roadmap meeting was in forty minutes.

She had the information. She just couldn't see it. It was like trying to read a sentence when all the words were in different rooms.

Her teammate Marcus knocked on the door frame. "Did you try that thing I sent you?"

She hadn't. It was in the same chat tool, in a different channel, sent on a Thursday, buried under a thread about the broken build.

She pulled it up.

Tidemark asked her to paste in what she had - the spreadsheet rows, the channel threads, the ticket notes. It read them. Then it handed back a single ranked list: the things customers kept saying, ordered by how often and how loudly they said them, with a shareable link her whole team could open.

The meeting started on time. The roadmap made sense.

That was what we built Tidemark to do.

Small teams run on the people who hold everything in their heads. When those people get too tired to hold it, the whole thing wobbles. Tidemark doesn't replace that judgment - it just puts the signal somewhere everyone can read it.

It launches next week. You can join the early list at tidemark.io.
