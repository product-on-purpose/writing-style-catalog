There is a kind of work that nobody sees. A customer clicks through the checkout page, the experience holds, and they have no idea that two systems ran in parallel for fourteen months while a team rebuilt the one they trusted, brick by brick, without ever letting the old one fall.

---

*"Let us not grow weary in doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up."*
Galatians 6:9

---

The word weary is the honest one. It is not the language of defeat. It is the language of cost. Fourteen months is long. There were two moments when the ground felt uncertain beneath the work - when late discoveries surfaced problems that threatened to reshape decisions already made and commitments already given. There was a launch date that moved, and then moved again. Anyone who has sat with a slipped timeline knows it is not merely a calendar inconvenience. It is the slow compression of confidence, the question that arrives quietly in the space between midnight and morning: *Is this going to hold?*

But this team did not give up. Priya and Dominic held a critical path together when the second near-miss surfaced a data integrity gap that nobody had anticipated, at eleven in the evening, without ceremony, while the rest of the organization was already somewhere else. They made the call and the work continued. Marcus spent three weeks on a migration sequence that most people on the outside will never know existed. These contributions do not appear on the launch announcement. The work they did was the kind that cannot be explained in a sentence to someone who was not there. But the passage names what they did: they did not grow weary. They kept going in the season before the reaping.

The final rollout held under peak load. That phrase carries more than it sounds like. It means the architecture was right. It means the decisions made under the highest pressure, at the moments of greatest uncertainty, turned out to be sound. The work that cost them so much was also the work that proved to be true.

There is something here for each of us who does work that is not visible from the outside. The patient, parallel labor - maintaining the old while building the new, holding the thread through a second near-miss and a second slipped date - this is a form of faithfulness. The scripture does not promise that the work will look impressive to the people passing by. It promises that the season of reaping arrives for those who do not quit.

This team did not quit. Today, let that be enough to sit with - not as a prologue to the next milestone, but as the thing itself.

---

What would it look like to receive this fully - not as a checkpoint on the way to something else, but as the harvest it is?

---

Lord, thank you for the people who stay. For the ones who hold the thread at eleven in the evening and show up the next morning to keep going. Let them feel today what they built, and let the weight of fourteen months settle not as burden but as proof. May the work that was invisible to most be seen, in this moment, for what it truly was. Amen.
