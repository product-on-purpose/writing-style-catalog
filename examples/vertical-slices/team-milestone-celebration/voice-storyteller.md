The night the first near-miss happened, Priya was still at her desk at eleven-thirty, watching the replication lag climb on a monitor she had taped to the wall with blue painter's tape. She had a cup of cold coffee she wasn't drinking. She had a sticky note on her keyboard that said *rollback? or hold?* in her own handwriting, like she hadn't trusted herself to remember the question.

She held.

That was October. The project had started the previous June, when the team lead, Marcus, had drawn a diagram on a whiteboard that nobody photographed because the meeting had run long and everyone assumed someone else would. The diagram showed two checkout flows running side by side: the old one, still taking orders, still generating the errors that had been bleeding the business for two years; and the new one, being built around it like scaffolding around a building that couldn't close for renovation. Fourteen months. Keep the lights on. Fix the thing underneath.

The launch slipped in January. It slipped again in March. In April, a junior engineer named Sorrel filed a ticket that turned out to be the reason the second slip happened at all - she had found a race condition in the session handling that the load tests hadn't caught. Marcus closed the ticket with a one-line note: *This one saved us.*

The final rollout ran on a Thursday afternoon in peak traffic. Dara, who had been on the team since the beginning and had taken exactly one vacation in fourteen months, watched the error rate on a shared screen in the open office.

It held.

Nobody made a speech. Someone ordered sandwiches. Priya refilled her coffee.

This is what hard work actually looks like from the inside.
