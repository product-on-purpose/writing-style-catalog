There is a corner of the third floor where the fluorescent light has flickered since the renovation. Howard taped a piece of yellow legal-pad paper to the wall beneath it in 2019, wrote "maintenance ticket #4,218" and drew a small box around the number, as if marking it for later. The light still flickers. The note is still there.

Nobody asked him to file the ticket. Nobody noticed when he did. That was mostly how it went with Howard.

The Tuesday after the Graymont account nearly collapsed - the routing error, the three clients threatening to walk - Marta found him at six-forty in the morning already at his desk, a mug of tea steaming beside his keyboard, a spreadsheet open on his second monitor.

"How did you even know?" she said.

He looked up. "Lydia sent me something last night. I figured."

He had pulled the records back eight quarters. Not because anyone asked. Because that was the thing that needed to happen, and he was the person who knew where the records were.

The account didn't collapse. Marta got the call from the client. She always said Howard got no credit for that one, and Howard always said that wasn't the point.

"The point," he told her once, stirring his tea, not looking up, "is that the client stayed."

Twenty-six years is long enough to become part of the building. Long enough that new people ask him where things are before they check the directory. Long enough that when he is gone, the directory will be all that's left, and it won't know what he knew.

He leaves on a Thursday. His desk will be clean before anyone arrives Friday morning. That is also, in its way, exactly like him.
