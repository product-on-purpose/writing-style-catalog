# We Will Not Do This Again

**A manifesto for institutional memory at Crestfield Operations**
Issued by Carolyn Marsh, Operations Lead, on behalf of Crestfield Operations - June 30, 2026

In the two months before Howard Thayer's last day, we tried to pull twenty-six years of judgment out of one person's head. We got the vendor contacts. We got the incident sequences, the escalation paths, the four utility contacts that had existed nowhere but Howard's own records. We did not get all of it, and no amount of exit-interview time was ever going to close that gap. This is not a story about Howard. It is a story about a department that let a single point of memory sit in one person for twenty-six years and called it normal operating conditions.

We are done calling it normal.

We believe institutional knowledge that lives only in one person's head is not knowledge the department actually has. It is knowledge the department is borrowing, on terms it does not set and a timeline it does not control, and the bill comes due the day that person gives notice.

We believe documentation is not a parting gift. It does not belong in someone's last two months, assembled under deadline by people who are simultaneously losing a colleague and racing a clock. It is a standing practice on a standing cadence, or it is not a practice at all - it is a scramble with better paperwork.

We believe mentoring is a function of the job, not a personality trait we happen to notice in hindsight. When someone spends two decades quietly keeping colleagues from expensive mistakes, that work has a name. The name belongs in how we describe their role while they are still doing it, not in the tributes we write once they are gone.

We believe a filled requisition covers a role. It does not cover a person. We will stop letting a completed hire stand in for an honest account of what this department loses when someone like Howard leaves, and we will say so plainly to the people above us, not just to each other.

We believe naming a gap out loud is a sign this department is paying attention. Pretending the gap does not exist, because admitting it feels like admitting failure, is the only actual failure available to us here.

Here is what we are done tolerating: heroics treated as an operating model, where one person's memory quietly catches what our systems miss and we call it a strength instead of a risk. Waiting for a resignation letter to discover a single point of failure we could have found on any ordinary Tuesday. Mentorship that goes unacknowledged for twenty years and gets a round of applause only at the goodbye party. A filled job posting mistaken for closure.

Here is what we are committing to instead: the quarterly knowledge capture practice adopted under ADR-0047 is now how this department runs, on a cadence that is not optional and does not wait for anyone's notice period. Mentoring is now an explicit expectation held by named senior individual contributors, whether or not it comes with a title. We write things down while the person who knows them is still here to check our work, not after. We tell leadership what a departure actually costs in the same conversation where we tell them the position is filled, and we do not let the second fact quiet the first.

So: every team lead's quarterly entry is due on the cadence, not on the day someone gives their notice. Everyone who was mentored by someone senior on this team, say so, in writing, while it still describes something you can point to instead of something you are trying to remember. And to whoever leads this department after this document is old news: measure what this team knows the way you measure what it ships. For twenty-six years those were treated as the same number, and nobody checked until Howard cleaned out his desk.

The next person who gives this department twenty-six years will retire from a department that already knew what it had. That is the only kind of goodbye we intend to give from here on.
