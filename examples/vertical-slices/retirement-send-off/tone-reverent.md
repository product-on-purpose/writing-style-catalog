There is a kind of person who does not accumulate titles, who does not chase the next role or the higher office, and whose value to an organization is precisely and entirely what they chose not to do with their ambition. Howard is that person. He spent twenty-six years in this building, most of them in the same chair, and what he built there was not a career ladder but something closer to a foundation.

He knew where the bodies were buried, as the saying goes, but that is too cynical a way to put it. He knew where the decisions came from. He knew which agreements had been made in which hallway in which year, and why they still mattered, and what would break if you forgot them. When systems failed and schedules collapsed and the pressure rose past what anyone had planned for, the people in that room looked for Howard. They always found him there.

He mentored without calling it mentoring. There are people at this company today whose paths through it were quietly shaped by a question he asked, a call he made, an afternoon he gave when he did not have to. He did not mark these things. He would not have thought to.

What leaves with him is not a set of tasks. What leaves with him is a kind of steadiness, a quality of presence that made the surrounding work feel grounded, that made this a place where something was expected to be done well, not merely done. There is no replacement in progress. There is no process document that captures what he carried.

We mark his departure because some things deserve to be marked.
