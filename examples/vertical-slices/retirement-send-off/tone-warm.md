Howard, you have been here longer than most people have known where they wanted to work.

Twenty-six years in the same role is not something that happens by accident. You chose it, year after year, because the work was worth doing and because the people doing it needed someone steady. That was you. When the system broke at the wrong hour or the deadline moved without warning, people did not panic - they found you. Not because you always had the answer, but because you made the problem feel survivable.

What most of the people you helped will not realize until later is how much of what they know, you gave them. You did not call it mentoring. You called it lunch, or a quick question, or "let me show you how this works." A dozen careers carry a piece of your patience in them, whether or not they know to name it.

We have been lucky to work alongside you. That is not a thing we say at retirement parties to be polite. It is specific: lucky that you stayed when staying was not glamorous, lucky that you cared about the right things at the right moments, lucky that you noticed the people around you well enough to know what they needed before they asked.

You are leaving the kind of absence that does not fill. We will keep your habits where we can. And we are glad - genuinely glad - that you spent this part of your life with us.
