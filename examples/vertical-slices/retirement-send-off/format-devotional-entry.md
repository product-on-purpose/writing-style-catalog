The conference room where Howard ran the quarterly planning sessions for most of two decades kept a particular rhythm. He would arrive before anyone else, his notes already written out by hand - not typed, written - and by the time the room filled in, the right question was already on the whiteboard, waiting. Not his answer. His question. He knew, in a way that is difficult to explain, that a room needed to work toward something before it was ready to receive anything.

---

"Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master."
Matthew 25:21 (ESV)

---

We tend to hear this verse through the lens of expansion. Faithful over a little, so - promotion. So - more. And for many servants in this parable, that is exactly what happens: a small trust becomes a larger one, and the servant is invited upward.

But Howard did not go upward in the way the organization chart would measure. He stayed. He came back to the same chair, with the same care, for twenty-six years. He learned what the company had been before most of us arrived, and he carried that knowledge the way a river carries the shape of the ground it has moved through - quietly, invisibly, but as the reason everything else flows where it does.

The parable does not say "faithful over a little and he moved on to something bigger." It says faithful over a little, and the master calls him into joy. The joy is not a larger assignment. The joy is the master's own. Howard understood this, I think. He showed up every day to something that looked small from the outside and held it with both hands.

The team has a name for what Howard carried: institutional memory. But that phrase is bloodless compared to what it actually meant. Institutional memory is Howard knowing, in the middle of a vendor dispute three summers ago, that the same clause had tripped up a predecessor relationship a decade earlier - and knowing where the old files lived, and knowing why that history changed what the team should say next. It is not a database. It is a kind of love, shaped like attention.

There is a version of faithfulness that waits for recognition, that accumulates a ledger. Howard did not appear to keep that ledger. He mentored people without naming it mentorship. He handed over credit the way another person might hand over a coat at the door - without announcement, without wanting it back. Several people in this building have careers they would not otherwise have, shaped by a question Howard asked them early on and then quietly followed up on three months later, not because he had to but because he had paid attention.

What do we do with an inheritance like that?

We cannot replace Howard. We can try to learn what he was practicing. The instinct to ask the follow-up question. The willingness to hold the room's question rather than rushing to hold the room's answer. The decision, made every morning for twenty-six years, that showing up faithfully to this particular work was enough.

Somewhere in this building right now, there is a question that only Howard knows to ask. In the weeks ahead, some of us will discover which question that is when we reach for it and find the space where he used to be.

Sit with that today.

---

*Lord, thank you for people who stay - who see that a faithful presence is its own calling, and who offer themselves to it without requiring an audience. Bless Howard in this next season. May he find in rest the same steadiness he brought to work. And may those of us who remain carry forward some portion of what he offered - not because we can fill his place, but because he showed us what it looks like to fill one well. Amen.*
