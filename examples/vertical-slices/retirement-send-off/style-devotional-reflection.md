The sticky note was still on his monitor when we cleaned out his desk. It read, in Howard's careful block letters: "Friday - budget meeting. Bring the Q3 numbers and the old contract from '08." He had written it three weeks before his last day, not knowing then that it would be the last note he ever posted there, and it struck me that this was exactly who he was: always leaning a little forward, holding both the present need and the history of the thing.

I have been turning that note over since.

There is a kind of faithfulness that does not announce itself. It shows up in the way a person answers the same question for the fourteenth time without any trace of impatience. It shows up in the handwritten annotation on a folder that nobody asked for but that someone, years later, will find exactly when they need it. Howard practiced this faithfulness the way a craftsman keeps a bench: not showy, not self-promotional, just maintained, day after day, because it was right to do so.

He was in the same role for twenty-six years. I have heard people mention this as if it were a kind of stillness, a stasis. But watch what happens in a room when a crisis arrives and someone turns, without thinking, toward a particular corner. That turning is itself a kind of testimony. You do not turn toward stillness. You turn toward bedrock.

Several people who work here - people who now lead teams, who now know what they are doing - might pause, if they were honest, and name the afternoon Howard sat with them and did not rush, did not give the quick answer, but asked the better question. They carry something of him. They may not always know it.

What do we do with a person who gave without keeping score? We are left, when they go, holding something we did not know we were receiving.

Howard's last day was a Wednesday. Someone brought a cake. He laughed at the card. He shook hands at the door and then he was gone, and the hallway was a little different than it had been, in a way that is hard to name precisely but that everyone who was there will recognize when they try.

I wonder if you know who your Howard is. I wonder if you have told them.
