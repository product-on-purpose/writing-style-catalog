Think about the last time something at work went sideways - a system failure, a project that no one could untangle, a meeting that was about to end badly. You know what you did. You called Howard.

Not because he was the most senior person in the room. Not because his title gave him authority over the problem. You called him because he had been in the building when that same thing happened twelve years ago, and he remembered what worked. You called him because when he sat down beside you, the room got quieter - not because he said anything brilliant, but because his presence communicated that the thing could be handled.

Twenty-six years. The same desk, mostly. The same phone extension that half the company had memorized without meaning to. He was not climbing; he was staying, which is its own difficult discipline. In a culture that reads upward movement as the only kind of faithfulness, Howard chose a different kind.

There is a phrase in Matthew 25 that gets quoted most often as celebration but carries something harder if you stay with it. "Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful with a few things." The word "few" is doing real work there. The faithful servant was not given everything. He was given a portion, and he was faithful with it - not because the portion was small, but because faithfulness is not measured by scope.

Howard's scope looked ordinary from the outside. From inside, you know it was not. Every career he shaped without asking for credit. Every person he steadied who went on to steady others.

What you are feeling now - that particular anxiety of not knowing who to call - is not weakness. It is the shape of genuine loss. He was here, and now he will not be, and that is hard to hold alongside the gratitude. Both are true. You are allowed to carry them together.
