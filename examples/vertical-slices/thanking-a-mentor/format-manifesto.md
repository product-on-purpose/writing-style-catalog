# Put Them Forward Before They're Ready

*A manifesto on developing people, and the ten-year-late thank-you folded inside it.*

Most organizations develop people by subtraction of risk. They wait until the track record already exists, then hand over the scope that would have produced it, and call the waiting patience. I do not believe development works that way, and I did not arrive at the belief through a training program. It was given to me, without my consent, ten years ago, by my mentor, Dana, who put my name forward for a project I had not earned and then refused to take it back when I tried to return it to her in a moment of real panic. I am done letting that gift sit unacknowledged. This is what it taught me to believe, stated plainly, in public, where a debt like this belongs.

**People become capable of a role by carrying it, not by being certified for it beforehand.** Competence proven in a simulation does not transfer to the room where the stakes are real. It has to be built there, under the actual weight, or it is not built at all.

**Staying close is not the same as staying in control.** A mentor who sits in the meeting and says nothing is doing something entirely different from a mentor who runs the meeting through someone else's mouth. The first is building a leader. The second is building a puppet with good attendance.

**Correction belongs after the room has emptied.** Said in front of the people whose respect a report is still earning, even a small correction becomes a public demotion. Said afterward, in private, it is only information, and information does not cost anyone their standing.

**The exposure of the bet belongs to the person who made it.** If the project fails, the question the organization asks is who vouched for this person, not why this person was not ready yet. A mentor unwilling to carry that question was never really offering the opportunity. She was only offering the title.

**A pattern that stops with you was never actually learned.** It was only performed. If you cannot hand it to someone else and watch it work in their hands, you were imitating your mentor, not practicing what she taught you.

I stand against the co-lead arrangement that grants a title while keeping every real decision in more senior hands, because it teaches nothing and protects everyone except the person it claims to be developing. I stand against the stretch assignment with an invisible ceiling, the kind that looks like trust from a distance and is actually supervision with a longer leash. I stand against correcting people inside the room where they are still building their nerve. I stand against mentors who wait for all the risk to disappear before they will hand off real work, and then call the waiting rigor.

I stand for nominating someone before the case for them is fully made. I stand for staying close enough to be asked a question and not one inch closer. I stand for absorbing the reputational cost of the bet myself, in full, without ever mentioning that cost to the person who is the reason for it. I stand for correcting once, afterward, in private, and only the part that actually needs correcting. I stand for stepping back on the day I am no longer needed, without making an announcement of having done so.

I am not proposing any of this for adoption. I am telling you it is already what I practice. This year I nominated someone on my team for something she did not yet feel ready to lead. I have stayed close. I did not take it back, not even in the week she tried to hand it to me. It is going the way it is supposed to go, which is to say it is hard, and it is hers.

Somewhere in the middle of watching that happen, I recognized whose hands I was using.

Dana, you did this for me a decade ago and never once asked me to notice what it cost you. I am noticing now, in the open, where it can be read instead of assumed. This document is the thank-you I should have written years ago, and it is also the only repayment a debt like this accepts: not gratitude spoken in private, but the pattern kept alive in public, in someone else's hands, on purpose.

If you recognize yourself anywhere in this, the ask is not complicated. Find the name of the person who put you forward before you were ready. Say it out loud, somewhere it can actually be heard. This practice does not survive on being felt. It survives on being named.

- Sable Marchetti
