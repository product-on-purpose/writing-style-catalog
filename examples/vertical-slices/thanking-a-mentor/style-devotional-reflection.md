There is an afternoon I have carried without knowing I carried it.

I was sitting at my desk when the email arrived - your name in the sender field, a thread I hadn't known you were composing. I read it twice before I understood what you had done. You had introduced me, to the whole team, as the lead for the Caldwell project. Not "will be working closely with," not "has been helping to shape" - lead. Present tense. Already decided.

I want to tell you that I felt ready. I didn't. I felt the way you feel when someone calls your name from across a room and every head turns - a sudden, clarifying awareness of where your hands are.

What you did next is the part that took me a decade to see. You didn't rescue me. You didn't take back the introduction or soften it into something smaller. You also didn't disappear. You appeared in my doorway that week and the week after, asking questions I could answer and questions I couldn't, and when I couldn't, you waited. You had patience of a particular kind - not the patience of someone who expects you to fail and is bracing for it, but the patience of someone who has seen something in you that you have not yet seen in yourself and is simply waiting for you to catch up.

I understand now what that cost. The project could have run faster without me learning on it. You had things to do. The patience wasn't free.

Last month I found myself writing an email introducing someone I manage to a set of stakeholders she didn't know. A project larger than she had run before. I felt you in my hands as I typed. I hadn't been thinking about you. I didn't plan it. But the sentence came out the way yours did - certain, clean, no hedge - and I realized I had been taught something I didn't know I had learned.

I don't know if gratitude like this lands right when it arrives ten years late. I suspect you would say something quiet about how it was never late, only timed. But I wanted to say it plainly: what you made possible wasn't just the project. It was the person I would become in the years after, every time I chose to trust someone the way you trusted me.

I keep wondering who taught you.
