There is a thing you did, Dana, that I did not fully understand until last month.

You put me forward for the Harmon account when I was not ready. I knew I was not ready - I told you so, in the conference room on the third floor, with early spring still gray outside the windows. You heard me out, and then you said my name was going on the brief, and that was the end of the conversation. What followed was six months of work I did not do cleanly or gracefully, work that required you to hold space for my uncertainty without filling it, to watch me reach wrong conclusions before arriving at better ones, to stay present without stepping in. That last part - staying present without stepping in - is something I have only recently understood the full weight of.

Last month I put someone I manage forward for a project she was not ready for. I watched her hesitate, and I heard the particular kind of doubt she named - not false modesty, but the real kind. And in the weeks that followed I found myself doing what you did: staying close without claiming the territory, holding back my own hand when everything in me wanted to reach in and steady what was tipping. It was harder than I expected.

So I am writing now because I understand something I did not understand then. You spent your patience on my uncertainty when you could have spent it on more certain things. You held open a space that you did not have to hold, and you held it long enough for me to grow into it. What that made possible I cannot fully name. I know it only by this: I was able to offer it forward, at last, because of how you once offered it to me.
