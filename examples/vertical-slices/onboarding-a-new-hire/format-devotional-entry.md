She arrived Monday morning with her laptop under one arm and a careful look on her face - the look of someone who knows how to learn but doesn't yet know where anything is. This is Priya. She is good at this, and she doesn't know you know that yet.

> "Then Boaz said to Ruth, 'My daughter, listen to me. Don't go and glean in another field and don't go away from here. Stay here with the women who work for me. Watch the field where the men are harvesting, and follow along after the women. I have told the men not to lay a hand on you. And whenever you are thirsty, go and get a drink from the water jars the men have filled.'"
>
> Ruth 2:8-9

Boaz sees Ruth working at the edges. She has arrived in a foreign field, in a land she did not grow up in, under circumstances she did not choose. She is working hard, and she is working alone. She does not yet know the shape of this place.

What Boaz does next is specific enough to become instructive. He does not offer a general welcome. He says: here is the field, stay in it. Here are the workers, follow them. Here is who to ask when you are thirsty. He answers questions she has not yet learned to ask. He provides access, orientation, a pairing, and a clear map of who belongs to what. And underneath all of it, he says something she will feel more than hear: I have already spoken for you here. You are not unprotected.

The ancient rabbinic tradition reads this moment as hesed - a word usually translated as "kindness" but meaning something closer to loyal love, the love that stays and shows up, the love that acts before it is asked. Boaz had no obligation to do any of this. Ruth was gleaning legally, but she was gleaning at the margin. Boaz moved her toward the center - not by granting her a rank, but by narrating the field until she could navigate it herself.

The first two weeks of a new role are a kind of gleaning at the margin. Priya is capable. She will find her footing even without your help, eventually. But "eventually" is not the same as belonging, and she will carry the memory of how it felt to arrive. You can set up her access, walk her through the codebase, pair with her on the on-call rotation, hand her a real change small enough to be safe and real enough to count. And then, when she opens the pull request at the end of week two, you can say: we were expecting that from you.

That last sentence is the water from the jars. It tells her she was not watched from a distance while she proved herself. She was expected in, from the beginning.

Who is standing at the edge of your field today, doing careful work at the margin, not yet knowing you have already spoken for them?

*Lord, give us eyes to see the ones who are arriving. Let us be the kind of people who answer questions before they are asked, who make room before it is requested, who name the ones who belong before they have to earn it. And when we forget - because we are busy and the field is large - call us back. There is always a Ruth in the field. Amen.*
