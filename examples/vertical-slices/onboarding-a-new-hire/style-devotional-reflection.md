She arrived at nine on Monday with a laptop bag and a careful smile.

I watched her scan the room - the way new arrivals always do - counting chairs, finding the coffee, locating the one face she had spoken with before. She did not yet know where the bathroom was. She did not know that the ticket tracker fills up fast on Monday mornings, or that the on-call engineer always looks slightly haunted by ten.

I noticed the smile first, though. Careful. A smile that does not ask too much, that has learned not to assume warmth will be returned.

There is something in that particular expression - the one that says "I hope I belong here" while performing a confidence it does not yet feel - that I have seen on my own face in mirrors, in other buildings, on other first days. The pretense of readiness. The hunger to be useful quickly enough that someone will want you to stay.

We sometimes call this an onboarding problem. We build checklists for it. Access provisioning, codebase walkthroughs, pairing sessions - all good, all necessary. But the checklist does not address what Priya was actually asking when she scanned the room: not "where is the coffee" but "is there a place for me here?"

The access gets set up. The codebase gets walked through. She pairs on a small change by Thursday of week two, and it ships, and it is real, and she is briefly thrilled and briefly afraid. These things happen with process.

What does not happen with process alone is belonging. That is slower work, and it does not live in the wiki or the ticket tracker or the pairing session, though all of those carry it when done with attention. It lives in the moment someone says, "That's a good question" and means it, or "Here's why we do it this way" instead of just "This is how we do it." It lives in the difference between being shown a codebase and being let into one.

Priya will learn to read the deployment logs. She will learn the unspoken rules about who handles which incidents. But will she learn that this team considers her an engineer and not yet another new hire to ramp?

That is the question worth sitting with, before any checklist begins.
