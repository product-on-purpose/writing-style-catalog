# The Guided Pairing Manifesto

Most engineering teams hand a new hire a login, a wiki link, and an unspoken test: figure it out, and we will decide later whether you belong here. We have watched that test run its course before - three or four weeks of quiet self-navigation, a first contribution that arrives so late it no longer feels like an introduction, a new engineer who is technically productive and still unsure whether anyone would notice if she asked for help. We do not accept that this is simply how onboarding works. It is how onboarding fails, slowly enough that nobody has to admit it.

## What we believe

**1. Belonging is engineered, not discovered.** A new engineer does not stumble into feeling like part of the team any more than a service stumbles into passing its health checks. Both are built, deliberately, by people who decided in advance what working looks like.

**2. A name beats a document, every time.** A wiki page cannot tell a new engineer which failure mode is real this week and which is a once-a-year edge case nobody has hit since launch. A named buddy can, because a buddy can be asked. Documentation is backup. It is never the plan.

**3. The first real contribution is a decision the team makes, not a milestone the new hire has to earn.** We scope it before her first day arrives. We do not wait for a new engineer to volunteer for real work, and we do not mistake silence for a lack of readiness.

**4. Verification is not optional.** "Should be done" is not the same as done. A checklist with no name attached to each line is a checklist nobody owns, and an unowned checklist is where new engineers go to get quietly stuck.

**5. Protection is not the same as delay.** Keeping a new engineer off the on-call rotation for her first 30 days is not a lack of trust in her judgment. It is sequencing, competence first and pager load second, and we will not apologize for choosing the order.

**6. The cost of doing this right is real, and hiding it is a failure of planning, not a kindness to the sprint.** A buddy who loses thirty to forty percent of their capacity in week one is not being generous. They are doing the job, and sprint planning should say so out loud instead of absorbing it as a rounding error.

## What we stand against

- The read-and-absorb plan that treats a wiki space as a substitute for a person.
- Silence as a design choice: the two, three, four weeks a new engineer spends unsure whether her question is worth someone's time.
- Measuring a new hire's first month only by what she produces, as if belonging were a side effect that either shows up or does not.
- Treating this sprint's velocity as more important than whether the person joining it ever becomes part of it.

## What we stand for

- A named buddy from day one, accountable for a checklist that is not complete until it has been verified with the new engineer directly.
- A first real change, scoped and waiting before the new engineer's first morning.
- Guided orientation before independent work, not instead of it.
- A check-in that asks about more than blockers.
- A name in the deploy log before the end of week two.
- Budgeting the buddy's time the way we budget any other real cost: in the open, on the sprint plan, not absorbed in silence.

## What we intend to do

Two weeks ago, Priya Rao started at this desk with a login, a checklist, and a person, Arjun Nair, whose job was to be asked questions. Three days ago, her first change shipped. No one had to guess when she would be ready. We decided that before she started.

This is not a pilot anymore. It is how Backend Services onboards every engineer who joins us, and we are done treating it as optional. If your team still hands new hires a login and a wiki link and calls it a plan, we are not asking you to take our word for the alternative. Name a buddy before the next person's first day. Scope their first real change before you know whether they will need help finishing it. Put the cost on the sprint plan where everyone can see it. Then watch what happens in two weeks.

We will not go back to guessing.

*Mei Chen, on behalf of Backend Services, Northlane Systems*
