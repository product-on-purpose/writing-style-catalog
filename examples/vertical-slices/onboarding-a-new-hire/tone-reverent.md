There is a threshold that every engineer crosses in those first two weeks, and the team that receives her either makes it real or makes it a formality. Priya is not yet part of this work. She will be, if the way she is brought in is handled with the care that the moment requires.

The first day is not a day for productivity. It is a day for orientation - the kind that lets a person begin to understand where they are. Access comes first, not because tooling is more important than welcome, but because standing without access in a codebase that is moving underneath you is a particular kind of helplessness. Set up her environments, walk her through the deployment pipeline, let her watch a deploy before she ships one. The codebase will seem vast. That is appropriate. It is vast. Give her a map, not a shortcut.

By the end of the first week, she should understand how the team makes decisions, who carries what, and where the on-call rotation places weight on people. Not every team explains this clearly. It should be explained clearly.

Week two is for the first real change - small by design, real in consequence. Not a contrived task, not a tutorial exercise, but something the system actually needs. Let her pair with someone who knows the relevant corner. Let her own the pull request. Let her see it reach production.

What she will remember from those two weeks is not the checklist of things completed. She will remember whether she was treated as someone whose arrival mattered. That is what belonging costs to give, and it is worth the cost.
