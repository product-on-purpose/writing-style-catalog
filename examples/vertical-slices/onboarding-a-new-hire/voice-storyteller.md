Priya's first pull request was nine lines.

That's where she started - not a ticket number, not a name badge, not a welcome email with seventeen attachments. The actual first thing: nine lines against the diff view on a Tuesday afternoon, two weeks after she walked in.

But let's back up.

She came in on a Monday. The laptop was waiting but the credentials weren't - not her fault, not anyone's exactly, just the usual gap between the HR system and the access-provisioning script that nobody had gone back to fix. Her first hour was three people in a row saying "let me check on that."

Marcus sat with her while the requests went through. Not to babysit her, just because he'd started eight months earlier and still remembered what it felt like to watch a progress bar with nothing useful to do. He pulled up the service map on his screen. Talked her through which services called which. "This one," he said, tapping the diagram, "this is the one that'll page you at 2 a.m. if you breathe on it wrong."

She laughed. A little uncertain.

By Thursday she had all her access, knew where the runbooks lived, and had watched two deploys go out over Marcus's shoulder. On Friday she picked up a task no one had gotten to: the error response that surfaced a raw exception instead of something a person could read. Small. Contained. Real.

Marcus reviewed her change. Left two comments, approved it.

She merged and deployed it herself, on a Tuesday two weeks in, her hand on the keyboard, watching the log output roll past. Nothing caught fire. The health check went green.

Later she told Marcus it was the deploy that made her feel like she was actually on the team.

Not the access. Not the welcome lunch.

The deploy.
