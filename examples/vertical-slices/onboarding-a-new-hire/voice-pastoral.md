There is a moment most engineers recognize but rarely name. It is Monday, the second week, and you have access to the repository but you are still afraid to change anything. You know enough to feel your ignorance, which is a worse feeling than knowing nothing at all. Priya is there right now. Your job is to meet her.

Ruth 2:8-9 records a moment of specific, un-sentimental welcome. Boaz tells Ruth: "Stay close to my workers. Watch the field where the men are harvesting, and follow along after them. Whenever you are thirsty, go and drink from the water jars the men have filled." This is not warmth in the abstract. It is a water jar, a row to follow, a field to stay near. The welcome is made of particular things.

Making Priya productive follows the same pattern. In her first three days, the work is access and orientation - not because credentialing is glamorous, but because a person who cannot log in to the ticket tracker or pull the repository is stranded in a doorway. Walk her through setup yourself. Sit with her while the environments come up. Let the questions that feel too basic come out.

By the end of the first week, pair her on one thin slice of the codebase. Not a tour. A real task - small enough to close in a week, real enough to matter. This is where belonging begins: not in the welcome email, but in the first pull request where someone says "this is good work."

You will not get this right every time, and neither will she. Some days the deployment will break and the on-call rotation will ring before she knows what she is defending. This is hard, and it is also true. What she needs from you on those days is not reassurance that it will be easy. She needs to know you are in the field beside her.
