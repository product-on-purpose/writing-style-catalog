# Recipe: coach-encouraging-decision

A composition of `coach` voice, `encouraging` tone, `socratic-inquiry` style, and `email` format. Used for helping someone reach their own decision instead of handing them yours.

## When to use

Someone you have mentored or managed asks you what they should do about a real fork in their life or career, and you can see that the durable answer has to be theirs, not yours. This recipe fits when the goal is to strengthen the reader's own judgment: you have perspective to offer, but offering it as a verdict would rob them of the reasoning they need. It is the right tool for career-decision emails, mentoring replies, and any moment where "here is what I would do" is the wrong gift.

## When to use something else

If the person has explicitly asked for your direct recommendation and the stakes or time pressure are high, an `executive` / `candid` answer that names a call respects what they asked for. If the question is factual rather than a values decision, a `technical-writer` / `matter-of-fact` reply that lays out the options serves better than questions. If you are not close enough to ask probing questions without presuming, a `diplomatic` one-pager keeps a respectful distance.

## Composition

| Axis | Entry | Why |
|------|-------|-----|
| Voice | `coach` | Develops the reader's own reasoning rather than prescribing an answer. Treats the decision as theirs to own and builds their capacity to make it. |
| Tone | `encouraging` | Builds the reader's confidence in their own judgment. Signals belief in them, which is what lets the hard questions feel like support rather than a test. |
| Style | `socratic-inquiry` | Teaches through well-chosen questions; the reader's reasoning is the point. Resists the slide into advice, which is the failure mode of this whole composition. |
| Format | `email` | One reader, a real reply to a real ask. Subject line, greeting, sign-off. The container for a personal answer to a personal question. |
