# Recipe: caregiver-warm-thankyou

A composition of `caregiver` voice, `warm` tone, `narrative-case-study` style, and `email` format. Used for personal notes of gratitude where you want the reader to feel the weight of what they gave, not just receive an acknowledgment.

## When to use

You want to thank someone who genuinely shaped you - a mentor, a manager, a teacher - and a generic thank-you would undersell it. This recipe fits when the gratitude is specific and the relationship is real: you can point to one particular thing the person did and trace what it led to. It is the right tool when the goal is to make the reader feel seen, not just thanked.

## When to use something else

If you are thanking someone for a discrete, transactional favor, a short `direct-communicator` / `matter-of-fact` note respects everyone's time better. If the message is public recognition rather than a private note, a `storyteller` tribute in `blog-post-long-form` reaches an audience instead of one inbox. If the relationship is formal and the note will be read by others (a reference, a nomination), trade the `caregiver` intimacy for a measured `senior-consultant` register.

## Composition

| Axis | Entry | Why |
|------|-------|-----|
| Voice | `caregiver` | Attends to the reader's experience, not just the writer's gratitude. Makes the note about what the person gave and what it felt like to receive it. |
| Tone | `warm` | Personal and present - the register of someone writing to a person they care about, not composing a reference letter. |
| Style | `narrative-case-study` | Anchors the thanks in one specific situation - what happened, the turn, the lasting result - so the gratitude is concrete rather than a list of adjectives. |
| Format | `email` | One reader, direct. Subject line, greeting, sign-off. The natural container for a personal note that lands in an inbox and gets re-read. |
