# Why I Stopped Using 1:1 Meeting Agendas

A few months ago I opened my shared 1:1 doc with one of my engineers, scrolled to the new heading I had pre-filled the night before, and watched her do the same on her side of the screen. Three bullets each. "Updates," "blockers," "career stuff." We had been running that exact template for almost a year. I looked at it and felt something I had been avoiding: the doc was the meeting now, and the meeting was the doc.

So here is the claim I want to make, and I will spend the rest of this post defending it. Most 1:1 agendas are theater. The good 1:1s are the ones with no agenda, or, more honestly, the ones where the two people in the room set the agenda live based on what is actually pressing that week. Templates and pre-filled bullet lists do not produce real conversation. They produce the feeling of preparation, which is a different thing, and worse, because the feeling lets us skip the real preparation, which is thinking about the person before you walk in.

## What the agenda was actually doing

Here is what I noticed when I started watching for it. The bullets in the shared doc were not the things either of us most needed to say. They were the things that were easy to write down two days in advance, when nothing was on fire. "Quarterly planning thoughts." "Status on the migration." "Career check-in." Topics that sound serious and never quite get to the real version of themselves.

The week one of my engineers was actually wondering whether to leave the company, the agenda still said "career check-in." The week the team was quietly losing faith in our roadmap, the agenda said "Q3 priorities." The week I needed to have a hard conversation about performance, the agenda said "feedback." The hard things hid inside the soft labels, and the soft labels gave both of us an exit ramp from ever actually getting there.

The agenda became a politeness instrument. Both of us could say we had a productive 1:1. We had hit our items. The boxes were checked. And we had successfully not talked about anything that mattered.

## Why the structure works against the meeting

Here is the warrant, the part I want to be explicit about. A 1:1 is not a status meeting. If it were, the agenda would help, because status meetings benefit from structure. A 1:1 is a relationship vehicle. Its job is to make sure the two people in it can speak honestly about hard things, fast, when they come up.

A pre-filled agenda quietly reframes the meeting as a transaction with deliverables. You came in with three items. We discussed three items. We are done. The structure tells your brain that the meeting is the bullets. So the things that did not make it onto the bullets, which are almost always the things that matter most, get filed as "off-agenda" - a category that, in any meeting that is running short on time, means "not today."

The other thing the agenda does is shift the work from the harder thing to the easier thing. The hard thing is thinking, before the meeting, about what is going on with this specific person in this specific week. The easy thing is filling in three bullets. The bullets give you the warm sense of having prepared, which then lets you skip the actual preparation. I did this for years. I want to be honest about that.

## What I do instead

I still keep a doc with each of my reports. I still take notes during the conversation. What I stopped doing is filling the doc out before the meeting. Instead, in the five minutes before we sit down, I ask myself one question: what is the most important thing I could say to this person today, and what is the most important thing they probably want to say to me?

That question does not always have an answer. Sometimes the honest answer is, "Nothing urgent, the work is going well, we should just check in." Fine. That becomes the meeting. We talk about how the work is going. We do not invent topics to fill the space. Quiet 1:1s are good 1:1s when there is genuinely nothing burning, and they cost almost nothing because we did not waste an hour the night before constructing an agenda for them.

When something is urgent, on either side, the absence of a template is a feature. There is no list of items competing for time with the real thing. The real thing is the meeting.

## What about the counterargument

The strongest version of the case against me goes like this. Without agendas, 1:1s drift. They become unfocused therapy sessions where nothing gets decided. The agenda is what protects against that. Engineers especially, who often hate small talk, need the structure to feel comfortable bringing things up at all.

I take this seriously, and I want to respond to it carefully because it is the version of the argument I used to believe.

Part of the answer is that drift is not the failure mode I see in most 1:1s. Avoidance is. Agendas are very good at protecting against drift and very bad at protecting against avoidance, and avoidance is the more common and more expensive failure. A meandering, awkward 1:1 that eventually gets to the real thing is a better meeting than a crisp, on-time 1:1 that politely never gets near it.

Part of the answer is that the reports who need structure to feel safe still get structure. They just generate it themselves, in the moment, by saying "I want to talk about X." That is the structure that actually matters, because it surfaces what is on their mind, not what they thought would look reasonable on a shared doc two days ago.

And part of the answer, the candid part, is that the agenda was never really for them. It was for me. It made me feel like a prepared manager. It let me walk in confident and walk out confident and not be sure, ever, whether the meeting had done its job.

## Closing

I do not think every team should burn their 1:1 templates today. If your current rhythm is working, leave it alone. But if you have ever closed a 1:1 doc and felt that low, ambient sense that nothing real had been said, I would suggest the agenda is not protecting you from drift. It is protecting both of you from the conversation you came to have. Try one week without it and see what surfaces. You can always go back. Most managers I know who try this do not.
