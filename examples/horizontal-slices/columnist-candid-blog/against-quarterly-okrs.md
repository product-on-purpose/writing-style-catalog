# The Case Against Quarterly OKRs

Every company I have worked at in the last decade has run on quarterly OKRs, and every one of them has spent the last two weeks of the quarter doing the same thing: gently negotiating which key results to call "green," which to call "yellow," and which to retire quietly so they do not show up red on the deck. We all know this. We all do this. Nobody talks about it, because the system has the look of rigor, and nobody wants to be the one to say that the look of rigor is not the same thing as rigor.

So I am going to say it. Quarterly OKRs are a bad fit for almost every team that runs them. They are too long for execution and too short for strategy. They incentivize sandbagging the targets at the start of the quarter so that the 70-percent completion benchmark looks heroic at the end. And they reset just often enough to disrupt continuity without ever forcing the harder annual or biannual planning conversation that the org actually needs. The quarterly cadence is a compromise that splits the difference between two useful things and ends up doing neither.

## What the quarter is too long for

Engineering teams do not plan in quarters. They plan in sprints, or two-week chunks, or "this thing we are building right now." The actual execution horizon is one to four weeks. By the time a quarterly OKR is set, the team is committed to it for twelve weeks, during which they will learn things that should change what they are building. They learn them in week three. They learn them again in week five. And then they spend the remaining nine weeks pretending those learnings did not happen, because the OKR is the OKR, and you do not change it mid-quarter without a good reason and an awkward conversation.

Or they do change it, quietly. The original KR gets rewritten in the doc, and now nobody can tell whether the team hit the target or moved the target. Both options are bad. The first is dishonest with the work. The second is dishonest with the score. The system forces you to pick one, and most teams pick the second because it is easier to defend in a review.

Twelve weeks is long enough for the world to change, but the OKR system treats it as short enough to commit to. That mismatch is the source of most of the cynicism that builds up around OKRs in any team that runs them for more than a year.

## What the quarter is too short for

At the same time, twelve weeks is not long enough to do anything strategic. Strategy operates on a horizon of one to three years. The questions strategy is supposed to answer - which markets are we serious about, which capabilities are we investing in, which bets are we willing to lose money on for a long time - cannot be expressed as a key result. They are too lumpy, too uncertain, and too dependent on choices that have to compound to mean anything.

So strategy at most quarterly-OKR companies gets pushed into the spaces between the OKRs. It lives in offsites and CEO blog posts that the operating cadence does not actually serve. The OKR system becomes a parallel reality of green and yellow and red boxes, ticking quarterly, while the real strategic conversation happens somewhere else, or, more often, does not happen at all, because the OKR ritual absorbs the planning energy of the org.

This is the deeper failure. Quarterly OKRs do not just fail to help with strategy. They crowd it out. Every hour leadership spends grading last quarter's KRs is an hour not spent asking whether the company is still aimed at the right thing.

## What the warrant is

Here is the underlying principle. Planning cadences have to match the rhythm of the work being planned. Standups are daily because that is the rhythm of "what is blocking me right now." Sprints are two weeks because that is roughly the rhythm of "what can we ship as a coherent piece." Annual budgets are annual because that is the rhythm of capital allocation. Each cadence is good for one specific kind of decision and bad for others.

The quarter is good for almost nothing. It is too long for tactical iteration and too short for strategic commitment. It exists mostly for historical reasons - quarterly earnings, fiscal calendars, the legacy of how MBAs were taught to run things in the 1990s. It is a default nobody picked, kept in place because everyone else did. The fact that quarterly OKRs feel awkward to almost every team that runs them is not a sign the team is bad at OKRs. It is a sign that the cadence is wrong.

## What about alignment and learning

The strongest version of the case for quarterly OKRs goes like this. The point of OKRs is not actually delivery. The point is alignment - making sure that everyone in the company can see what everyone else is trying to do - and learning, the deliberate practice of setting an ambitious target, missing it, and reflecting on why. Judging OKRs on whether teams hit them, the argument goes, is judging the wrong thing.

I want to take this seriously. The people who say it have read the original Andy Grove material, and they are not wrong about what OKRs were supposed to do.

But here is the honest picture. In every company I have seen actually run OKRs, the system that gets implemented is not the alignment-and-learning system. It is the grading-and-performance-review system. The framework arrives as Grove's and gets implemented as Welch's. The KRs end up in the performance review. The score ends up in the comp committee deck. And the moment that happens, the incentives invert and the whole system runs on sandbagging.

You can argue, correctly, that this is not the fault of OKRs themselves but of how orgs bolt them onto existing performance systems. Fine. But if a tool is reliably implemented badly across nearly every org that picks it up, at some point the tool is part of the problem. The quarterly cadence makes it worse, because the short loop means there is always another grading event around the corner, always another moment to shade the score.

## Closing

I am not against goals. I am against the specific ritual of grading them every twelve weeks. If your team is actually using OKRs as a learning instrument, where the score is irrelevant and the conversation about why a KR moved or did not move is the actual product, you are in the rare two percent and you should keep doing what you are doing. For everyone else, the honest move is to admit what the quarterly OKR has become inside your org, name the gap between the framework and the practice, and ask whether the cadence is worth the cynicism it generates. Most of the time, when I have asked that question carefully, the answer has been no.
