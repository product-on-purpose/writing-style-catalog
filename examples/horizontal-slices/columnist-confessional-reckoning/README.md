# Recipe: columnist-confessional-reckoning

A composition of `columnist` voice, `confessional` tone, `classical-argument` style, and `blog-post-long-form` format. Used for personal essays that turn a hard, lived experience into an argument the reader can carry.

## When to use

You have been through something - a failure, a hard year, a decision that did not go the way you hoped - and you want to write about it publicly in a way that earns more than sympathy. This recipe fits when the vulnerability is the credential: the honest account of what happened is what gives you the standing to argue the lesson. It is the right tool for year-end reckonings, "what I learned" essays, and post-mortems of personal bets, where the point is not catharsis but a claim you want others to believe.

## When to use something else

If you only want to process the experience for yourself, a private `devotional-reflection` serves better than a public argument. If the lesson is technical or professional rather than personal, a `pragmatic-architect` / `candid` `decision-log` keeps it grounded in the work. If you want to tell the story without arguing a thesis, drop `classical-argument` for `narrative-case-study` and let the events speak.

## Composition

| Axis | Entry | Why |
|------|-------|-----|
| Voice | `columnist` | A first-person writer with a stake and a point of view, willing to be quoted on the conclusion. The voice that earns the right to generalize from one life. |
| Tone | `confessional` | Honest about the writer's own failings and feelings. Supplies the vulnerability that makes the argument land rather than preach. |
| Style | `classical-argument` | Gives the essay a spine - claim, reasons, a counterargument taken seriously, conclusion - so the reckoning arrives somewhere instead of circling. |
| Format | `blog-post-long-form` | A title and the space to move from story to argument and back. The container for an essay meant to be read by strangers, not a journal entry. |
