# Recipe: storyteller-celebratory-announcement

A composition of `storyteller` voice, `celebratory` tone, `narrative-case-study` style, and `blog-post-long-form` format. Used for announcements that deserve to be felt as well as read - where the milestone has a real story behind it and the audience earned the right to know it.

## When to use

You are announcing something that took long enough to matter. The team has a before and an after, and the gap between them involved real struggle. You want readers to feel the weight of the accomplishment, not just register the fact of it. This recipe fits product launches, shipped migrations, and team milestones where the story is the point and the announcement is a vehicle for the story.

## When to use something else

If the announcement is time-sensitive and the audience needs the facts first, a `direct` voice with `precise` tone carries the news faster. If the goal is to persuade skeptics rather than celebrate with believers, a `problem-solution` style in `pragmatic-architect` voice has more argumentative structure. If the audience is internal leadership and the milestone needs context on business impact, a `stakeholder-update` format lands the relevant numbers without the narrative arc.

## Composition

| Axis | Entry | Why |
|------|-------|-----|
| Voice | `storyteller` | Carries meaning through scene and concrete moment. The accomplishment becomes real when it has named people, a specific day, and something that almost didn't happen. |
| Tone | `celebratory` | Names the specific thing that was hard and makes space for it before pivoting forward. Sincere rather than promotional - the reader finishes feeling that the achievement was accurately seen. |
| Style | `narrative-case-study` | Opens in the before-state, moves through the turning point, lands in the after. The principle arrives last, earned by the story. Forces specificity: not "things improved" but what specifically changed. |
| Format | `blog-post-long-form` | Gives the story room to breathe and the reader navigation. Headers let someone who knows the team skim; prose rewards someone who wants to understand. The closing carries a takeaway, not a summary. |
